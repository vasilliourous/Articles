Are you ready to dive into some more fun homelabbing projects for this weekend? Well, you’re in luck, because I have three fun (and easy) projects for you to try out, including building your own web archiving platform, keeping up with your investment portfolio, and building a custom homelab push notification system.

                        Back up your favorite websites locally and automatically with ArchiveBox
               

            The internet is disappearing, so you should do something about it
    
There are parts of the internet that I wish I had saved forever ago, but I didn’t, so they’re gone forever. Sure, the 
Wayback Machine
 exists, but it’s not perfect and there are definitely times when it doesn’t actually have a website (or version of a website) that I want, but 
ArchiveBox
 fixes that.
With ArchiveBox, you can set your homelab up to 
automatically back up various parts of the internet
, from (public) Facebook or Instagram photos to content from YouTube or Soundcloud. You can also set it to download the public portions of entire websites.
That’s really the key limiting factor to ArchiveBox—it can only download things that are public, and it’s a snapshot in time of what the website is displaying. It doesn’t keep live links to databases to continually show updated info when you visit, it’s just purely a snapshot from when it last archived the website.
✕
 Remove Ads
Just know that you’ll definitely need a decent storage system to handle backing up larger websites or videos, so 
make sure you have a NAS deployed in your homelab
 before going too crazy with ArchiveBox.

                        Monitor your investment portfolio in private with Ghostfolio
               

            Know your return on average investment and more without sacrificing privacy
    
Outside of health privacy, I view financial privacy as one of the most important aspects of my life that I don’t want anyone else knowing about, including other companies. 
Ghostfolio
 is designed with that thought in mind, but from the perspective of keeping track of your investments and their performance.
With Ghostfolio, you’ll be able to manage all of your wealth assets like creating, updating, or deleting transactions, seeing the ROAI (return on average investment) for today, week to date, month to date, year to date, and more. The analytics that Ghostfolio provides can help you identify and manage risks within your portfolio.
✕
 Remove Ads
Another great feature of Ghostfolio is the fact that it can work as a 
progressive web app
 with a mobile-first design, so, if you put it behind 
a reverse proxy
 (or 
Cloudflare Tunnel
), then you can add the website to your phone to keep up with your investments from anywhere.
So, whether you have a large investment portfolio already or are just starting, be sure to deploy Ghostfolio in your homelab today so that way you can always know what your investments are doing. Now might be the perfect time to spend some overtime cash on a few new stocks, but without some type of monitoring system, you’d never know it.

                        Add custom notifications to your homelab with ntfy
               

            No more silent failures
    
Credit: Lucas Gouveia/How-To Geek | tsingha25/Shutterstock
 Have you ever sat there and wondered, “When was the last time my system backed up?” only to find out that backups had been silently failing for weeks? I have, and 
ntfy
 can help with that.
✕
 Remove Ads
With ntfy, you can set up 
a custom self-hosted push notification service
 that runs inside of your homelab to let you know when various things happen. For instance, you can set up notifications whenever your system starts, completes, or fails a backup. Having something like this configured would allow you to know if your system is properly backing up or not.
Another use case could be knowing when a Docker container updates (or when updates are available), getting warnings on disk space, CPU, or RAM usage, and much more.

            You don’t need to spend a ton of money to start a homelab
    
You might be looking at other people’s homelabs and think, “That’s just unobtainable by me, it’s too expensive to homelab.” But you’d be completely wrong.
It’s actually quite easy to 
start a homelab from scratch without spending a single dime
. Simply grab one of the old computers likely sitting in your closet or office and get Linux installed. Even if it’s a 
really
 old computer, it’ll likely be a great starting point for a homelab.
✕
 Remove Ads
So, if you’ve been putting off trying any of these weekend projects because you don’t have the cash to buy a new computer to start a homelab, stop letting that get in the way and just use what you have.
