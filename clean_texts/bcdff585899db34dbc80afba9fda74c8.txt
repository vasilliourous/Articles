I used to think of 
my old PC
 as “still useful,” which is a polite way of saying it was headed toward the tech shelf of retirement. It handled everyday stuff fine, but it didn’t feel like it had a second act. Then I finally listened to our resident Proxmox guru, 
Ayush Pande
, and installed Proxmox. Now, my tiny Geekom Air12 Lite mini PC is something closer to shared infrastructure than a personal computer. Once it clicked, I stopped seeing limits and 
started seeing possibilities
.
✕

Old hardware becomes more valuable when it’s predictable.
The biggest difference is how quickly I can 
spin up isolated environments
 to test ideas. I run several Linux VMs and LXCs for different scenarios, and I keep a Windows 11 VM around for the tasks that still expect it. Even my recent pearOS install started life inside Proxmox, where curiosity is safer and cleanup is easier. When a lab makes experimentation cheap, you experiment more.

                        Why Proxmox fits old hardware
               

            It makes “good enough” feel modern
    
Proxmox is great at giving older hardware a job that matches its strengths. Instead of chasing the newest desktop features, the machine becomes a stable host that mostly stays out of the way. That’s the kind of workload an aging PC can actually excel at, especially if it has decent RAM and reliable storage. It doesn’t need to be flashy, it needs to be consistent.
It also helps that Proxmox is opinionated in the right ways. You get a web interface that doesn’t feel like a toy, and you get a hypervisor foundation that’s proven and widely understood. The result is a platform that encourages you to build a lab responsibly, even if you’re experimenting a lot. Old hardware becomes more valuable when it’s predictable.
✕
 Remove Ads
Once Proxmox is in place, the PC stops being a single environment you’re afraid to mess up. It becomes a host for many environments, each with its own purpose and lifespan. That shift is what makes the setup feel like a “real” home lab instead of a pile of installs. You’re not rebuilding your base every time you want to learn something.

                        VMs that keep experiments contained
               

            Linux testing without collateral damage
    
Linux VMs are where most of my testing happens, because they’re the easiest way to explore without consequences. I can evaluate distributions, packages, kernels, or desktop choices without risking my daily workflow. If the experiment goes well, I keep the VM around and refine it. If it goes poorly, I delete it and move on.
That matters because experimentation has a hidden tax: recovery time. A normal install invites half-permanent tweaks, and those tweaks pile up until you’re not sure what “default” even means. A VM keeps the mess in a box where it belongs, which makes troubleshooting faster and results more honest. It’s easier to compare two distros when neither is weighed down by years of personal baggage.
VMs also let me isolate scenarios rather than stack them on a single system. I can keep a baseline VM for clean testing and a separate one for “what happens if I do something reckless.” When I want to recreate a bug, I can keep a frozen copy of the environment that produced it. That’s the kind of discipline that’s hard to maintain on bare metal.
✕
 Remove Ads

                        LXCs that earn their space
               

            Lightweight services with clean boundaries
    
LXCs are where Proxmox starts feeling like a practical home lab tool, not just a virtualization platform. A container is often lighter than a full VM, which makes it a great fit for small services. If a utility doesn’t need its own virtual hardware, an LXC keeps the overhead low. You still get isolation, but you’re not paying for a whole guest OS experience.
The bigger win is how containers encourage clean boundaries. Instead of building a single VM that runs five services and becomes precious, I can split things up. One container does one job, and I can upgrade or replace it without touching the rest. That separation makes the lab 
easier to maintain
 when the fun part wears off.
Containers also make it easier to keep your lab tidy as it grows. When a service misbehaves, you know exactly where it lives and what it depends on. You don’t have to dig through unrelated config just to find the culprit. Proxmox rewards that kind of organization, which is rare in hobby setups.

                        Windows 11 without taking over
               

            Compatibility when you need it most
    
✕
 Remove Ads
I run a 
Windows 11 VM
 because reality still occasionally demands it. Some tools assume Windows, some vendors only ship Windows utilities, and some workflows are just easier to validate there. I’d rather have Windows available than scramble when a random task becomes urgent. Proxmox gives me that safety net without turning Windows into the center of my setup.
Running Windows as a VM also keeps my main environment cleaner. I’m not dual-booting, and I’m not dedicating another machine to a handful of occasional needs. The VM can be updated on my schedule, tested after updates, and rolled back if something breaks. That control is the difference between “useful” and “annoying.”
It also helps when I want to test mixed-environment behavior in a realistic way. If I’m evaluating a Linux service stack, I want at least one Windows client that behaves like a normal user machine. A VM is perfect for that because it’s consistent and repeatable. You can validate assumptions without turning your personal computer into a test subject.

                        pearOS inside a safer sandbox
               

            Curiosity thrives when cleanup is easy
    
pearOS is a good example of why Proxmox became my default starting point. When you’re evaluating something new, you want a buffer zone that allows the experiment to be reversible. A VM gives you that freedom, especially when a distro has bold defaults or unusual design choices. You get to explore it on its own terms without committing your whole machine.
✕
 Remove Ads
It also changes the tone of experimentation. Instead of thinking, “Do I really want to install this?” you think, “What do I want to learn from this?” That’s a healthier mindset, because it keeps you focused on outcomes rather than fear of consequences. Proxmox makes curiosity feel normal, not risky.
And when you decide an experiment is over, the cleanup is honest. You can delete the VM and know it’s gone, not half-gone with leftovers hiding in the corners. That matters when you do a lot of testing across Linux VMs and LXCs. A home lab stays fun when it doesn’t turn into permanent clutter.

                        It’s not all fun and games, though
               

            Sprawl and complexity can sneak in
    
Proxmox 
makes it easy to build
, which also means it’s easy to overbuild. Storage choices matter, networking can get intricate fast, and it’s tempting to create clever setups that don’t actually serve you. Ten VMs can be perfectly reasonable, but it can also be a fast lane to resource contention if you’re careless. The platform gives you power, but it doesn’t stop you from making a mess.
I’m quickly reaching a point where just the one Proxmox server may not be enough. I’m either going to need to build another node to cluster or seriously upgrade my mini PC’s hardware. I can hardly complain, though. This tiny beast still has just the 256GB NVMe SSD it shipped with, so it was probably time to upgrade that months ago anyway.
There’s also the slow creep of sprawl. You create “just one more” VM for testing, then keep it because you might need it later. Soon, you have a small ecosystem that needs updates, backups, and naming conventions that don’t collapse under their own weight. It’s not a dealbreaker, but it’s something you have to manage deliberately.
✕
 Remove Ads
Windows VMs can amplify those pressure points, too. Give Windows too little, and it feels miserable; give it too much, and it steals comfort from everything else. The solution is to treat resources like a budget, not a wish list. Proxmox works best when you right-size workloads instead of maxing them out.

                        What my home lab became
               
Proxmox didn’t just let me run ten VMs at once, it gave my old PC a job that fits how I actually work. I can test Linux distros and service stacks without risking my daily systems. I can keep a Windows 11 VM around for the moments when compatibility matters, without letting it dominate my setup. Most importantly, I can try things like pearOS in a safe sandbox and keep the lab tidy when the curiosity moves on.
