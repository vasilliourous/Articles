I often write articles about common home lab pitfalls and containerization mistakes you should avoid – and most (if not all) of them come from experience. After all, tinkering with server distros and virtual guests involves a bunch of variables, and all it takes is one argument in the wrong config file to reduce a project to a broken mess.

Heck, although I have a couple of years under my belt as a home labber, I still end up modifying the wrong network bridge or causing a virtual machine to become unresponsive from time to time. But as long as you don’t mess with certain high-risk security settings, you don’t need to fret about following every single guideline outlined by the home lab community.

                        Take your time choosing the right OS for your experimentation arsenal
               

            You don’t have to jump into the deep end right away
    
If you’ve been checking out home lab forums, blogs, or tutorials, you’d think 
Proxmox
, 
ESXi
, and other virtualization environments are essential for computing experiments. While I’ll admit that they’re a solid starting point, you can work on projects on just about any setup. For folks who find typical virtualization environments rather complicated, you can start things off with a simple Linux distro and KVM, and work your way up from there.
This might sound like heresy for a guy who often talks about dedicated virtualization platforms, but I daresay that you can get into the home server ecosystem with something as unorthodox as Windows 11. Throw in a type-2 hypervisor like VirtualBox/VMware Workstation Pro, and you can work with Linux distros without configuring dual-boot on your primary machine. I’d recommend switching to 
Hyper-V
 as soon as possible, but you can take your sweet time getting familiar with all the toggles, menus, and options in your hypervisor.
✕
 Remove Ads
Alternatively, if you find the prospect of self-hosting FOSS services more enticing than experimenting with random distros, you can deploy CasaOS, 
Cosmos
, or other containerization platforms inside a virtual machine. That way, you can learn about containers without jumping into CLI commands right off the bat.
Once you’ve grown familiar with the management UIs on typical hypervisors, you can finally move on to typical home lab platforms. This journey may take you a couple of weeks (or even months), but it’s way better than racking your brain over a virtualization environment when you’ve never deployed a VM and losing all your enthusiasm in the process.

                        Don’t be afraid of making mistakes when working with new services
               

            Breaking your app stack is a learning experience (and a rite of passage)
    
The Ollama LXC failing to connect to the GTX 1080
Let me be clear: when I mention mistakes, I’m talking about random accidents that can cause your home services to break down, not something as catastrophic as letting strangers on the Internet access your tinkering hub (and I’ll go over that in a bit). Let’s say you’re experimenting with a virtual machine and ended up making it unresponsive. You might get cold feet after bricking a VM, but I’m here to tell you there’s no need to get discouraged by a failed project.
✕
 Remove Ads
Heck, I’ve had useful self-hosted services break down when I tried integrating other tools (mostly LLM services and plugins) into the mix. With a few snapshots, restoring my virtual guests was a piece of cake. But even without dedicated backups, I’d just jump back into the fray after sighing loudly, as breaking things is a part of the learning experience.
After all, a home lab is essentially a safe haven where you can tinker with cool projects, make a couple of mistakes, and then fix everything. Time and again. If you’re too cautious about your home lab, you won’t really be able to experiment to your heart’s content.
Likewise, although it's a good idea to set up HTTPS certificates for your services and lock your nodes behind MFA tokens, you don't have to work yourself to the bone following these rules when you're just a beginner. They're definitely useful when you've got a powerful home lab catering to multiple users, but they won't do you any good if you get burned out trying to set them up in your days as a fledgling tinkerer.

                        But whatever you do, please don’t expose your home server to the Internet
               

            And try to document your experiments
    
While you should work with your home lab at your own pace, there are a couple of tenets you should always keep in mind. Opening random ports on your router, for example, is something you should avoid at all costs, as it will expose your private tinkering environment to hackers, botnets, and other malicious entities lurking in the endless recesses of the Internet.

You’ll also hear many home labbers (including yours truly) harp on about the importance of backups. If you’re just starting out and don’t have enough money to spend on a second rig, you can skip regular backups. But it’s still a good idea to snapshot essential virtual guests, as it will make recovering from failed projects a lot easier.

Finally, I’ll recommend documenting every little experiment, networking tweaks, and virtual guests in your arsenal – and you can go with good ol’ pen and paper if you must. That way, you’ll always have some resources to fall back on when troubleshooting a broken virtual guest. Trust me, my random scribbles save me a lot of headaches when I have to fix random services in my convoluted mess of a home lab.

I tend to get annoyed by the people saying  "don't expose to the internet" without any "unless" or proper explanation.
Of course you can expose your server to the internet, but only if you know what you're doing. That means ensuring you have a solid firewall, and as few ports open as possible, ideally, the only port you should need should be 443, with an isolated reverse proxy handling the rest. Even better, test your server, attempt to hack into it yourself, search for vulnerabilities, just think of it as a little game. 
But, if you don't have time for any of that, then indeed, do not expose it to the internet.
