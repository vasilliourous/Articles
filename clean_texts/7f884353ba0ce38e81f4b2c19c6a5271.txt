GNOME aims to be simple to use, with a minimal interface, a small set of core apps, and basic customization available by default. But GNOME extensions can offer almost anything you might miss from a fuller-featured desktop environment.
✕
 Remove Ads
Many of these extensions understandably relate to window management, but there are also many widgets and small utilities available. You’ll also find extensions to tweak preferences, including many of those that have proven divisive within the community.

                        Apps menu
               

            An old-school launcher perfect for Windows users
    
 GNOME 3 removed the Applications menu, which, much like 
the Windows Start Menu
, provided a quick way to launch apps. While apps can be launched in a variety of ways, many users prefer to browse hierarchical groupings; if you do, you can restore the menu with the 
Apps Menu
 extension.
Apps Menu adds a small menu to your top panel which opens a list of categories, including Accessories, Games, and System Tools. It offers no configuration, however, so you’re stuck with a pretty minimal offering. The extension is part of Classic Mode, meaning that GNOME officially supports it, which should guarantee some level of stability.

                        Caffeine
               

            To keep your display awake no matter what
    
 Some extensions are so simple, it’s a surprise they’re not built into GNOME already, but that’s the whole point—and benefit—of the extension system. 
Caffeine
 performs a trivial task, but you’ll be grateful for it when you need it.
Simply put, Caffeine stops your machine from going to sleep, just as its name implies. Click on its icon, and you’ll instantly turn off the auto-suspend and screensaver features. Click again, and you’ll turn them back on. The extension’s icon updates to reflect its current state although, with monochrome minimalism, it can be difficult to notice until you’re used to it.
Even though it does very little, the extension comes with some useful settings, including a very convenient list of apps that automatically keep your system awake when you open them.

                        Coverflow Alt-Tab
               

            Switch windows like it’s the 2000s all over again
    
 For a few years in the late 2000s, when iPods and iTunes were big news, Cover Flow wowed us with its smooth, pleasing animation of album covers flying horizontally at the flick of a finger. Or mouse, when it made the leap to OS X, and beyond with countless imitations.
If you want to wallow in nostalgia, or if you’re just a fan of this particular UI device, you’ll love this extension, which does the same thing for windows. 
Coverflow Alt-Tab
 offers plenty of configuration, with the ability to control the easing, its speed, the icon size, and much more. The animation is smooth, and seeing full-sized windows when switching is definitely a bonus.
✕
 Remove Ads

                        Clipboard Indicator
               

            Manage a crowded clipboard with ease
    
 Expanding your clipboard beyond a simple single-entry store, this extension will help you juggle snippets of recent text that you’ve copied, extending their lifespan and saving you time. On installation, you won’t notice an immediate difference because Clipboard Indicator gets out of your way until you need it, which is when you have something in your clipboard.
Copy some text, and you’ll see the extensions icon appear in your top panel. Pressing this reveals a menu of your previous 15 pieces of copied text, which you can select from to re-copy. You can also clear the clipboard or delete individual items when you’re done with them. Again, there’s no further customization here, but this simple utility performs a very useful task without overcomplicating it.

                        Just Perfection
               

            Nobody’s perfect, but tweaking settings can get your desktop close
    
 Unlike the other extensions on this list, 
Just Perfection
 doesn’t provide a targeted tweak or specific function; instead, it expands the configuration of GNOME significantly.
The extension presents a few high-level profiles for generalized settings, including a “Super Minimal” option that hides nearly every GNOME element. With customized settings, you can control a whole range of interface details, from the appearance of the clock or system menu to the enabling of type to search and popup delays.
Just Perfection can help you tweak GNOME to your heart’s content, restoring much-loved features that have been sidelined or picking the perfect combination of icons and menus to suit your needs.

                        Tiling Shell
               

            When you want things to line up, just so
    
✕
 Remove Ads
Tiling window managers
 are popular with power users with big monitors who like to view as much as possible, all at once. They help you to organize apps in grid-style alignments, discouraging the use of overlapping windows. GNOME avoids tiling by default, but you can experience it with this extension, 
Tiling Shell
.
Everything is driven by Layouts, which let you predefine the areas of your screen that windows can occupy. Using Layouts, you can control both positioning and sizing, then simply drag windows into those preallocated slots. When you start dragging a window, a panel near the top of your screen becomes available. Simply drop a window onto your preferred Layout, into your chosen slot.
Editing Layouts can be a little tricky at first, but the intuitive approach with a full-screen overlay makes the process as simple as possible, and the extension is generally a joy to use.

            To peek behind the curtain
    
 Transparency in desktop interfaces is a controversial topic: some people like it, some cannot stand it. You may even resort to [wrestling with settings to turn it off altogether], on a system like macOS Tahoe.

But fans of semi-translucent stuff should check out the Transparent Window Moving extension, which wisely sticks to making windows transparent only when you’re moving them. On larger displays, with many overlapping windows, this can be a great help but, let’s face it, this extension is mainly an aesthetic one, to add a bit of visual interest to your desktop and show off your processing power. In those terms, it’s definitely a success!

                        Window List
               

            The two-for-one extension that brings back memories
    
✕
 Remove Ads
 Another extension that caters to fans of a particular desktop UI feature, 
Window List
 displays a thin panel at the bottom of your screen with a button for each window open in it, much like the Windows taskbar.
There’s minimal configuration, but you can set a grouping policy (for apps with more than one window) and decide how multiple monitors and workspaces are handled. Talking of workspaces, the extension also adds a workspace switcher in the bottom right, a feature that pays off for anyone running more than a few apps at once.

            There’s so much more to see
    
There are 
hundreds of GNOME extensions available
, so it’s worth your time to explore them. Use popularity and star ratings to find the best, and pay attention to notes about incompatibilities. Above all, have fun trying out what’s on offer!
