I'm not gonna lie. As I keep tacking on services, both those that I'm using every day, and those that I install just to tinker around, my homelab has been getting messier by the day. And I've reached a point where it's no longer just a fun quirk of my setup, it's a genuine practical problem. And it's a simple problem at that. With the sheer number of services running, I simply don't know what's running, what's broken, or where things are anymore. It doesn't help that my 
home lab
 is segregated across multiple servers. So, this weekend, I decided to tackle this problem.
✕

I installed Homepage because I wanted a single place to see everything in my home lab. Not logs or metrics, those I can still extract through one of the many tools I have installed. Instead, I was looking for a clean, well-designed, human-readable overview of my stack that I can glance at and understand immediately. Homepage has solved that problem completely.

                        A clean front-end for your entire stack
               

            See your entire stack at a glance
    
Ease of use is important to me, and Homepage ranks among the easiest installs I've come across in the 
self-hosted
 world. A simple stack file is all it takes to get the service up and running, with the rest of the configuration done entirely through YAML files. What I like about Homepage is how straightforward it is. For one, it's not a full monitoring solution. It's a front-end for my self-hosted infrastructure. Unlike tools that try to do it all, I prefer that delineation between services. Do one thing, but do it well.
All you have to do is define your services, group them in a way that makes sense to you for your needs, and attach useful context to each one. That could be as simple as an icon and a link, or a live status pulled from an API. There are a lot of configuration options here.
✕
 Remove Ads
Talking about configuration, like I mentioned, everything runs through configuration files, much to my delight. 
YAML
 keeps things simple. Every time I add a new service, I document it and back it up, and it's a cinch. It's effectively a map of my entire stack.
Service widgets elevate the experience further. Instead of just listing a service, Homepage can indicate whether a plugged-in service is working. It can pull real data, reflect the current status of the app or service, and give you a glanceable signal if something's not working as you expect it to. Essentially, instead of jumping between individual tools and services, you can open one page and get a snapshot of the entirety of your stack.
Since Homepage integrates deeply with Docker, things get even better. It can read directly from the Docker socket and automatically surface container information. This means you don't need to manually keep everything up to date. Just label your containers, and Homepage will pick them up. The Docker labels integration is extremely nifty and does a lot to reduce the heavy lifting usually associated with setting up dashboard-like services.
And finally, the speed of access is critical for a service like this. There's no point in setting up a home page dashboard if it's quicker to just launch a service directly. Just like my other favorite, 
Dockpeek
, Homepage is exceptionally fast and easy to get to. I keep it open anytime I'm at my desk, and it's set up as my start-up page in Chrome.
✕

                        Easy monitoring without the overhead
               

            A quick way to know if everything's working as expected
    
 The biggest difference, as always, is in how Homepage changes your approach towards basics, like monitoring your home lab. More specifically, what all do you monitor, and how often? It doesn't replace the obvious tools like 
Grafana
, or give you deep logs. That's not the goal here. It's more about surfacing the information you need to act on immediately. Usually, I'd keep multiple tabs open, check containers, glance at metrics, and put things together. Now, I just open Homepage. If a service crashed overnight, I see it right there. Usually, everything is fine, but when it's not, I don't find out after the fact when I might get back to the service in a day or a few.

The layout is also simple, but effective. It's flexible enough that I can easily organize things based on how I think. It's not particularly rigid. Combined with things like icons and widgets, it takes a visual approach to monitoring that I much prefer. When I switch on my laptop first thing in the morning, I don't want to look at data. Just tell me if things are working as they are supposed to, so I can move on with my day.
Homepage isn't just a monitoring dashboard either. You can also use it to launch other services. Just start typing, and it'll surface the service. It's an easy way to launch lesser-used services without having to deal with a mess of bookmarks or a list of IP addresses.
✕

            A simple dashboard I can't live without
    
Homepage lies squarely in the nice-to-have list of services, but it's these services that add a degree of polish to any home lab. It surfaces and shows me the things that I need and use every day. It helps me monitor my most 
critical containers
 and services to make sure that they are running as expected. However, it's a simpler kind of monitoring. Just enough to know that something is running or not. It's become my home page, quite literally. And, in my opinion, it's an easy recommendation for any home labber who hasn't set up a dashboard yet.
