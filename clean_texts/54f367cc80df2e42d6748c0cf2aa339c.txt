How to boost your Linux skills beyond a beginner level
Credit: Lucas Gouveia/How-To Geek
By 
Graeme Peacock
Published
 Apr 18, 2026, 10:30 AM EDT
Graeme Peacock is a seasoned Linux expert with more than 15 years of hands-on experience. He has worked extensively with Ubuntu, Gentoo, Arch Linux, Qubes, and Fedora, gaining deep proficiency in everything from routine terminal operations to highly customized system builds.

Graeme began his journey with Ubuntu, quickly mastering the command line and essential system administration skills. A year later, he moved to Arch Linux, where he spent nearly a decade refining his expertise through the installation and configuration of multiple minimalist systems. After some time, he moved to Gentoo, where he configured and compiled both server and desktop environments using normal and hardened profiles and frequently compiled custom kernels. Graeme moved to Qubes in 2016, where he has remained ever since.

Graeme has extensive experience with highly configurable tools such as Vim, Neovim, and Emacs, and he maintains his own complex configurations. He is also highly proficient with Bash, Zsh, and dozens of utilities.

Graeme holds a B.S. in software engineering and has a strong passion for programming and web development. He is proficient in Golang, Python, Bash, JavaScript, TypeScript, HTML, and CSS. He also has considerable experience with Docker and is currently working on learning Kubernetes.

                        Try a project
               

            Learning is doing
    
Practice makes perfect, as they say, but I'd go one further and say that it's 90% of learning.
I assume you already have the basics nailed down: booting Linux, starting and stopping applications, and rudimentary terminal skills.
Gaining significant experience takes years if you only partake in everyday Linux use. You want to fast-forward that and challenge yourself—you need a goal, like a project.

            Ideas for projects
    
Choosing a project shouldn't be difficult; think of something you need, and systematically pursue it. Some ideas are:

                                        File server (
NAS
).
                        

                                        An advanced router with a transparent VPN.
                        
DNS server
, possibly with ad-blocking (e.g., 
Pi-hole
 or 
DNSCrypt
).
                        
Media server
.
                        

                                        A network intrusion detection system (NIDS) that passively monitors all network traffic (e.g., 
Suricata
).
                        

                                        Self-hosted cloud features (like 
Nextcloud
). You can use this to sync your cell phone's pictures, contacts, and more.
                        
We have tons of ideas in our self-hosted category, so if you're struggling for inspiration, be sure to check it out.

            Approach
    
If your system is powerful enough, and you're up for the challenge, you can host multiple servers on the same system with 
KVM
 (virtual machines). However, that's not strictly necessary, and you can run multiple services on the same machine without it. Alternatively, use a 
VPS
.
✕
 Remove Ads
Setting up services (like Nextcloud) is much easier these days with technologies like 
Docker
. You no longer need to make all the different variables align with your distro, because many of its images work with little fuss.
Make sure to run Docker in 
rootless mode
 or use 
Podman
 instead.

            Choose a distro
    
Next, 
choose a distro
 you're comfortable with. Since 
Docker Hub
 hosts official images for most services, your choice really boils down to preference. 
Debian
 and 
Ubuntu Server
 are easy to recommend, but it's your choice. I'd strongly recommend something without a graphical environment, because we shall use 
SSH
 to control it.
Installing the server is where your project begins, and if you encounter any problems, be sure to keep notes (more on that in the last section). Keep in mind: you'll almost certainly need a keyboard and monitor to begin with.

            Set up SSH
    
Credit: Lucas Gouveia/How-To Geek
 Once you've installed your chosen distro, set up SSH. Think of this as a mini-project. Most server-based systems come with SSH pre-installed and possibly configured to run when you boot the computer. I recommend using 
key-based authentication instead of a password
—it's much more secure and convenient.
However (if possible), having physical access (with a keyboard and monitor) gives you room to make mistakes, because misconfiguring SSH can lock you out of your system.
✕
 Remove Ads
Once you have SSH access set up, you're now ready to do everything else remotely.

            Quickly write a roadmap
    
At this point, you should have a primary goal defined, but you may not know the steps to get there. I strongly suggest making a project-specific page in your notes and using it as a temporary workspace.
To begin with, write down your ultimate goal—like setting up a DNS server—then use 
reverse-goal setting
 to discover the intermediate goals. For example, setting up an Unbound DNS server may look something like this:

                                        Configure nftables rules
                        

                                        Configure Unbound
                        

                                        Make Unbound accessible to the network
                        
That's a start. Now, ask questions about how to achieve each goal, and reformat your list

                                        Configure nftables rules
                                                

                                        Enable external DNS port access
                        

                                        Apply the rules at boot
                        

                                        Make Unbound accessible to the network
                                                

                                        Set a listening port
                        

                                        Enable the service at boot
                        
Now, keep iterating upon that list until you run out of ideas—do research, take notes, and improve it. This is how I avoid tutorials entirely; I make my own while learning in the process.
It's difficult to imagine every problem you'll encounter up front, so don't spend longer than 10–20 minutes defining goals. Once you execute the plan, you'll discover any gaps. Just be sure to 
document your journey
.

                        Automate with Bash
               

            A few hours of learning will carry you far
    
If you're setting up a server, there's no way to avoid the shell. You can get quite far with Bash by knowing a few key concepts—
if statements
, 
functions
, 
variables
, and 
exit codes
. You don't need long and complicated scripts; in most cases, 1–10 lines are sufficient.
Most scripts you write will be wrappers around complex commands, or they'll launch something via a daemon (
unit file
).
Python or Perl are perfectly suitable alternatives.
✕
 Remove Ads
The basics of Bash should only take an afternoon. As before, define your end goal and use reverse goal setting. Remember to take notes.

                        Document your journey
               

            Notes help generate ideas and solve future problems
    
In recent years, note-taking has become invaluable to me. I can't undertake any moderately difficult project without a workspace (project-specific notes) to record my thoughts, lessons, solutions, goals, and progress. If you've got more than one thing going on in your life, you'll need to switch context frequently, which causes you to forget small details, increasing the difficulty. While doing it without project-specific notes is possible, you'll miss out on long-term retention and next-level organization.

Here are the different things I record, either under a subheading or in a separate (but linked) note:
Thoughts
 (subheading): These are potential project ideas, things that inspired me, or similar things I've previously done. This serves as context to put things into perspective. More important than it sounds.
                        
Lessons
 (separate notes): Problems I've overcome, and their solutions. They're links to separate, long-term notes that include a summary. This keeps all my achievements relevant for the duration of the project.
                        
Goals
 (subheading): Computing this upfront gives me direction, builds a clear overview, and keeps my mind free from confusion. These live front-and-center in my project notes, and they're often a list of to-dos.
                        
Progress tracker
 (subheading): A temporary section in my project notes that's a detailed description of what I have just done and what I need to do. It's usually 1–2 paragraphs, and it helps me pick up where I left off, even after months of absence.
                        
Journal
 (separate notes): Keep track of what you did, what you learned, what your problems were, how you overcame them, and what your weaknesses are. You should review these frequently (or at the end) to guide further learning.

My project notes are a temporary workspace, and the main page has fewer than 1000 words—which includes thoughts, goals, progress details, and more. These are things I don't mind losing if I delete the project. Everything else I record in permanent notes, which includes my lessons and journals, which are invaluable for self-analysis and a long-term knowledge base.
You can find more details on 
my note-taking process
 in another article I wrote. I used 
Org-roam
 (Emacs) for years, but now I use 
org-node
. These packages allow me to link notes together (like the World Wide Web). They're not for everyone, and 
Obsidian
 or 
Logseq
 provide the same benefit with much less difficulty upfront.
The most important things are that you're making mistakes, finding solutions, and recording your findings using a highly organized note-taking system. These three activities are how you build upon prior experience without it fading. As you administer, maintain, and build new projects, your skills will naturally become stronger and build much faster through tough challenges. Having all the relevant things on hand accommodates and reinforces learning.
Your results don't need to be perfect; let time and experience take care of that. It may take months to build your first project, but the process is what counts. You're leveling up your skill; remember that.
✕

If you take nothing else away, take this: Learn through doing, and always refine your note-taking system.
