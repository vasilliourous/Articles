                        Wait, but what's KVM-over-IP anyway?
               

            Think of it as a mini-computer to control your bigger computer
    
Normally, 
KVM switches
 take one mouse, keyboard, and monitor(s) and share those peripherals between multiple computers. That's the general idea of KVM-over-IP, except that instead of sharing between two physically connected PCs, one of them is elsewhere, connected over the network.
While you can do this 
with a Raspberry Pi
, I wanted something that would work even if my network went down. Yes, that's a disturbingly common occurrence in my home lab, completely self-inflicted whenever I mess up something with the router settings.
✕
 Remove Ads
Now, I'm already using a few 
GL.iNet
 devices as portable routers, and they sent over their newest KVM-over-IP device, the Comet 5G. This KVM works over Ethernet, Wi-Fi, and also 
5G RedCap
, ensuring that I'll have connectivity to my home server for admin tasks no matter what. Well, okay, if the local cell towers go down, I've got another problem, but that'll be larger than my home lab.

            Ex-enterprise hardware often has out-of-band management built in
    
Hardware used in the datacenter have 
dedicated out-of-band management hardware
 to give you console access from the second the machine goes on. That could be on your servers, or the network appliances, but all of it is easily configurable from a dedicated network that works whether that machine is on or not, and often provides a view into the boot sequence as well.

                        My home lab has never been easier to manage
               

            Being able to see what happens during reboots is fantastic
    
Every remote access tool I've used before this fails as soon as the client device goes offline, or if the local network links do. That's a big problem in my home lab, because if it's not DNS this time, it'll be DNS the next, and I lose visibility over my hardware all too often.
✕
 Remove Ads
With 5G RedCap, Ethernet, and Wi-Fi 6 options for connectivity (and yes, I have all three enabled and working), if the wired link goes down, I have two other connectivity options, and there's always the 5G connection unless I lose power and my UPS runs out of capacity. The Wi-Fi radio can be flicked into hotspot mode for use in areas with zero connectivity, which is also a fantastic feature.
Being able to manage my server as if I'm sitting next to it is fantastic, and stops me from having to scramble to find cables and other devices if the worst happens. I've even used it for BIOS updates, since you can upload files to the Comet 5G and make them appear as local storage. You can even use it to control a Fingerbot if you need to use a physical power button, and it also supports Wake-on-LAN if your device's BIOS supports it.

            Oh, and it can be a Tailscale node
    
I've got it connected to my tailnet, for even more security (and for remote access without using the GLKVM app), but I could use Zerotier for a similar setup. It all really depends on your home lab, and the best devices offer options without limiting you to a single ecosystem.

            Having KVM-over-IP on my home server makes my life easier
    
There's a reason out-of-band management is a big deal for enterprise use: we homelabbers normally have to deal with decommissioned servers or huge energy bills to achieve the same thing. Having a reliable KVM-over-IP device attached to my server means I can tweak BIOS settings or fix things if it won't boot, when I'd have to walk over to it and plug in equipment to fix things otherwise. That saves time and effort, and I've started using it for normal management tasks as well, so I don't have to worry about SSH not reconnecting or any of the myriad of other issues I often cause.
