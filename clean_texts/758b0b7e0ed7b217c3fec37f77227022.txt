                        What's the difference between PCIe Gen 3 and Gen 4?
               

            The gap is bigger than the name suggests
    
Credit: Behnam Norouzi / Unsplash
PCIe is the interface that connects your SSD and GPU to the rest of your system. Gen 3 and Gen 4 are two successive generations of that interface, and the difference in available bandwidth between them is significant.
It is also worth knowing that PCIe Gen 5 NVMe SSDs are already available if you are looking to go even faster. Gen 5 x4 has a theoretical ceiling of around 14,900 MB/s, roughly double that of Gen 4.
A PCIe Gen 3 x4 connection gives your SSD a theoretical sequential read rate of around 3,500 MB/s. PCIe Gen 4 x4 roughly doubles that, pushing it to around 7,000 MB/s. So if you have 
bought a high-end Gen 4 NVMe
, running it at Gen 3 speeds means that it can't reach its full potential. If that's a little too technical and filled with jargon, think of it as owning a 240Hz monitor but running it at 60Hz because you forgot to change it in 
Windows settings
.
✕
 Remove Ads
The frustrating part is that nothing warns you about this. The SSD will show up, Windows will boot normally, and everything will work smoothly. You’ll only find out about this issue when you actually transfer large files and notice it's taking longer than it should — and that was the exact case for me.

                        How to check if your SSD is being held back
               

            Your SSD's report card is one benchmark away
    
Credit: Robin John / MakeUseOf
The easiest way to check is to download CrystalDiskMark (
free
) and run a quick sequential read/write test. Look at the top two rows labeled SEQ1M Q8T1 and SEQ1M Q1T1. Those are the numbers that reflect raw sequential throughput.
Credit: Robin John / MakeUseOf
If your drive is a Gen 4 NVMe rated for 6,000 MB/s or above and your results are closer to 3,000 MB/s, the BIOS setting is almost certainly the reason. You can confirm the rated speed by searching for your exact drive model name. Most manufacturers list the sequential read and write specs prominently on the product page. In my case, I was getting 3,651 MB/s read and 2,839 MB/s write on a drive rated for up to 7,300 MB/s. That was enough for me to dig into the BIOS.
✕
 Remove Ads

                        Fixing this is almost insultingly simple
               

            From 3,000 MB/s to 6,000 MB/s
    
For this, you'll need to get your hands dirty and enter the BIOS. You are looking for something labeled 
M.2 PCIe Mode, M2_1 Gen Mode, PCIe Speed, or M.2 Slot Configuration
. It will typically show options for 
Auto, Gen 3, and Gen 4
. If it is set to Gen 3 and your board supports Gen 4, that is the problem.
On my MSI Z890 Gaming Plus Wi-Fi motherboard, I found the settings under 
Advanced -> PCIe/M.2 Configuration
. This should be the same for most ASUS, Gigabyte, and ASRock motherboards.
If you cannot locate it, search for your exact motherboard model name with something like "PCIe M.2 BIOS setting," and you will land on either the manual or a forum thread that points you to the right menu.
Here is the full process from start to finish:
1. 
Restart 
your PC and enter the 
BIOS
. Depending on your board, this is usually the 
Delete, F2, or F12 key
 during startup. The correct key typically flashes on the screen for a brief moment before Windows loads.
2. Navigate to your 
advanced settings
 and look for 
M.2 or PCIe configuration
 options.
3. Find the 
PCIe generation
 setting for the slot your 
SSD 
is installed in. If you are unsure which slot it occupies, head to the Easy Mode section of your BIOS — it will show you the exact location. Look for labels such as 
M2_1, M2_2, or M2_3
 to identify which one to target.
4. Change the setting from 
Gen 3 to Gen 4
, or switch it to 
Auto 
if that option is available.

5. 
Save 
the setting and exit. The PC will 
restart 
on its own.

            Worth checking before you blame the drive
    
Credit: Samsung Memory / Unsplash
After switching to Gen 4, both sequential read and write speeds improved significantly. The SN850X was finally running the way it should. I had been getting less than half of that for no reason other than a default BIOS setting I had never thought to check.
If you have a Gen 4 NVMe and your transfer speeds seem suspiciously slow, check the BIOS before assuming the drive is at fault. It takes less time to check than it does to open a support ticket. And while you are in there, it is worth checking any other M.2 slots too if you have multiple drives installed. The same default can affect all of them.
