Subscriptions are part and parcel of modern day entertainment, streaming, and even software use. But I had no idea how much money I was wasting on 
subscriptions
 until I started to track them down and make a note of things. Turns out, it took a deep dive into my email receipts to find every single 
streaming service
, SaaS tool, domain, random trial that I've never bothered canceling. While, yes, the cost of these services certainly matters, not having visibility is a bigger issue. I only really noticed many of these services when I got charged, and then too, often the merchant name doesn't match the actual service.
✕
 Remove Ads
Let's just say, it's a real problem, which is why I set up Zublo. This 
self-hosted
, open-source app gives you a single, local dashboard for every recurring expense that you make, and gives you a proper look at exactly what you're paying for.

                        Visualize your spending
               

            Fully private and offline spending analysis
    
Credit: 
 Zublo's entire focus is on giving you visibility into your subscriptions. The idea here is that you add in whatever subscription you want to track, the cost of it, the renewal data, the moment you start a subscription and Zublo then gives you a dashboard to track it month on month. It effectively turns a rough idea of what you're paying for into precise numbers, timelines and context.
The set-up is straightforward. The app is easy to install via 
Docker
. Just use the Compose file provided on GitHub. Once done, start adding each subscription one at a time, including how often it renews and how much it costs. Zublo supports multiple currencies, so if you've got multi-currency subscriptions, that's no issue at all. From there, it auto updates the dashboard. I like that it isn't replacing a full-fledged budgeting tool. It just tracks your recurring payments.
✕
 Remove Ads
The 
main dashboard
 gives you a quick glance at what you're paying, monthly and yearly. That alone is extremely useful when you're trying to save money. But the real value comes from identifying which subscriptions are costing you the most. That Tidal subscription I opted in to try out, turns out I barely ever used it.
There's also the fact that not every subscription renews on a monthly basis. For some subscriptions, I end up taking up a quarterly renewal, and for others, yearly. Emails are not an effective way to track them. Zublo aligns all of them into a single view so I can see what's coming up next instead of being caught off guard. This lets me cancel the service preemptively instead of being charged for it.
The calendar view is another big help. I can scroll through upcoming renewals and immediately spot anything I might want to cancel. You can see a breakdown of spending over time. This lets you analyze patterns in your spending. Since all your subscriptions are categorized, you can see if you're spending too much on entertainment, or tools that aren't adding much value. That helps you remove guesswork from your decisions. And all of that runs on your own 
NAS
, which means, it goes without saying, your data stays entirely private.

                        Why specialized tools win
               

            Ditch the spreadsheet
    
Okay, so you might be wondering why you need to spin up an entire service when a 
spreadsheet
 can do the same. Yes, it can. But a purpose-built tool that gives you a cohesive look and an easy way to add all that data.
✕
 Remove Ads
Instead of manually entering all fields in an Excel sheet, I can tap the new subscription button, add in data, pull a logo, and billing cycles as necessary. That's an important change for me, because if logging something feels effortless, I'm more likely to use it every time.

Like most services these days, you'll find 
AI integrations
 here. You just have to provide an API key and a base URL for the service provider you want to use, and the tool can give you AI-powered insights into your subscription habits. It works, but it's not something I use. For one, the regular dashboard gives me enough data as is. But beyond that, the point of self-hosting it to keep this data private. I'm not very comfortable with the idea of sharing my financial habits with an LLM. But that's just me. And yes, before you ask, it does work with local LLMs too.
Finally, if you want to go the AI route, you'll even find a built-in chat assistant that lets you inquire about your subscriptions using AI, or add new ones without tapping the few buttons needed.

            An essential tool for subscription management
    
Zublo isn't the most expansive software, but that's not an issue for me. It works because it solves a very specific problem and does it well. It's not trying to manage your finances or become a full-fledged 
budgeting tool
. The goal here is to give you insights into recurring expenses and let you act on them. It's helped me cut down on subscriptions I no longer needed, especially trials I'd signed up for and forgotten to cancel. And I have much better insights into my monthly recurring spends. To me, that alone makes it worth keeping this installed in my home lab stack.
