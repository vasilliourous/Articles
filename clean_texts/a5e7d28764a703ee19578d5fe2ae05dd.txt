                        Real time visibility into containers and exposed ports
               

            Port sprawl becomes unavoidable in self-hosted setups
    
Port sprawl, or the over-abundance of container ports to sort, is something practically every 
self-hoster
 runs into. Between services, you're testing out and essentials like Immich, Karakeep, Home Assistant and others, it's effectively hard to keep track of these. If you are particularly well organized, you'll give them local domain names, but not everyone does that, or has the know-how. The other way, of course, is to just keep a series of bookmarks or even a spreadsheet documenting the specific ports. That's a no-go either the moment you install something new, or if an IP configuration changes. What I wanted was real-time visibility into my stack and the ports being used. Which containers are running, which ports they expose, and that's the gap that Portracker fills for me.
✕
 Remove Ads
Portracker runs as its own container and watches the host that it lives on. The service inspects running containers, looks at open ports, and gives you an easy-to-understand interface from where you can see exactly what port each service on your 
NAS
 is using. You don't need to make any manual input. Whenever you add a new container to your stack, it automatically shows up here and if you stop or remove a port, it gets removed. Simple as that. That alone greatly simplifies the process of keeping track of services on your server.
While you can always check Docker or Portainer to find out what ports are being used on your system, that can be borderline overkill for an otherwise simple and straightforward task. Portracker, gives you a single point interface to glance, spot and launch your choice of container.
One of the biggest advantages here is, again, the glanceability of your self-hosted stack. All too often, I've discovered that I've been running a wide array of services that I set up to test and completely forgot about. Portracker gives you a running list of every container on your system, and just that. This lets you easily isolate services that you don't need, remove them, and free up resources.

                        How Portracker fits into my everyday setup
               

            A single place to see what is actually running
    
✕
 Remove Ads
In my own setup, Portracker has become immediately useful. I run everything on a 
Synology NAS
 with Docker on a dedicated volume. Between media services, backup tools, admin dashboards, and one-off experiments, I've got dozens of services running and ports occupied. With Portracker running, I open one page and see the current state of the system. No guessing or context switching is needed. This has helped me massively in removing and clearing up unused services from my system. If something remains on my system, its instantly visible to me.
Portracker offers advantages too. For example, it's got a built-in 
port generator
. So, when I deploy a new service, I can instantly ask for a recommendation. Earlier, it meant scanning through my existing services to see what ports are being used. Every once in a while, the stack would fail to deploy because I was reusing the port. Portracker solves that problem entirely. It sounds like a minor benefit, but it removes a lot of friction in everyday use, which I'd say is half the point of running your own set of services in the first place.
Finally, an understated advantage is troubleshooting. When a service doesn't load up, I immediately see if it's a networking issue, or something up with the app. If the port is open and visible in Portracker, that means the service is deploying, and the issue is with the app. If not, it's likely a deployment issue.
✕
 Remove Ads

            Portracker is the first port tracking tool that worked for me
    
I've tried many tools that claim to show you your ports or network maps, but Portracker fits my own use case extremely well. On one hand, it keeps the use case straightforward and sticks to the core function of revealing what containers are running and what ports are being used. Alongside, it adds some nifty features, like the ability to generate an empty port. I still end up documenting parts of my setup, but day to day use, where I'd end up relying on bookmarks or spreadsheets to locate where my service was, Portracker has completely taken over, making it one of the most useful 
self-hosted
 services on my stack.
