I’ve been using Evernote since 2016, when I was a junior in high school. It’s been a reliable place to store notes, ideas, and journal entries. It handled most of what I needed, but it was never a perfect fit.
While I had used it on and off, I recently decided to start using it more seriously. However, after all these years, a few things made me want to explore other note-taking apps that better matched my current needs. I eventually landed on Notion, and it fills the gaps I had with Evernote.

                        Why I finally quit Evernote
               

            The reasons behind my departure from the Evernote user base
    
After years of 
using Evernote
, it was time to move on. While it wasn’t an easy decision, several factors ultimately led me to make the switch.

            Restricted to one notebook
    
I don’t want to pay for 
a note-taking app
, especially when there’s already one built into my device. Still, I originally 
switched to Evernote
 because it offered better organization than the default Notes app.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
 However, 
the free plan isn’t perfect
. It comes with several limitations that seemed manageable at first, but became more frustrating the more I used the app.
One of the biggest issues is the restriction on notebooks. On the free plan, you’re limited to just one notebook, which quickly becomes a problem as your notes grow.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
 I prefer to keep different notebooks for different topics, similar to how I would with physical notebooks. While I started with just one notebook in Evernote for creative writing, my interests have expanded, and I now want separate notebooks for each.
✕
 Remove Ads
I’ve been able to organize my notes using titles and a specific structure to manage multiple topics within a single notebook, but that only goes so far. Having separate notebooks would make things much simpler.

            Maxed out at 50 notes
    
On top of that, the number of notes you can create is also limited. With the free plan, you can only create up to 50 notes before hitting the cap.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
 When I first started writing, I only created one note a week as a summary. But as my interests grew and I wanted to write more, that limit quickly became unrealistic.

            Ads to upgrade are everywhere
    
The final straw, however, was the constant push to upgrade. Ads seemed to appear everywhere—whether I was trying to create a new notebook or simply typing, with one sitting above the keyboard.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
 At every turn, there was another prompt to upgrade. It disrupted my train of thought and made it harder to stay focused while writing.

                        Notion gave me everything I needed for free
               

            It gave me back the features that I missed with Evernote
    
When looking for a new app for notes and journaling, I focused on what wasn’t working in Evernote and what I could get for free elsewhere. I knew I wouldn’t get the most feature-rich option, but what I needed wasn’t too much to ask for.
✕

Credit: Patrick Campanale / How-To Geek
 After browsing, downloading, and testing different apps, I 
settled on Notion
 as my new writing app and moved everything I had in Evernote there. Luckily, transferring my existing notes was easy, though it had to be done through Notion’s desktop site rather than on mobile.

            No limit on how many notes you can create
    
One of the main reasons I switched to Notion was the lack of a cap on the number of notes I could create on the free plan, unlike with Evernote. This allowed me to write daily journal entries instead of just weekly ones, without worrying about hitting a limit.

            Template marketplace within the app
    
This is especially useful when browsing the wide range of templates created by others, with some free and others priced. These templates cover many different use cases and topics, and unlike the Evernote app on my device, I can browse a marketplace of them.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
 For example, my notes used to look the same no matter the purpose—just a title, a block of text, and a few scattered photos. With templates, I can use one specifically for fitness entries and another for journaling about emotions.
✕
 Remove Ads
Furthermore, some of these emotion-focused templates include prompts that help guide reflection and provide a starting point during moments of writer’s block. They’ve been especially helpful when I know how I’m feeling but struggle to put it into words.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
 Writing about different topics without worrying about limits has been a major benefit. Just as important, you can separate those topics so they’re not all in the same space. You can organize them, sort them, and easily collapse or expand sections as needed.
This makes it much easier to reference past entries without scrolling through multiple notes in the same notebook. I know exactly which section to check, and the search feature makes it even easier by letting me find specific content within my notes.

            More aesthetic customization options
    
Another feature I appreciate is the added level of customization beyond just names and content. Not only can I label each note and section, but I can also add an icon and a cover image—something I could do in Evernote, but it looks better in Notion.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
✕
 Remove Ads
 These additions give each note more personality and provide extra context if the title doesn’t fully explain it. They also make the app feel more visually engaging, rather than just a screen filled with text.

            Access your notes from more than one device
    
To top it all off, these features can be used across multiple devices. Unlike Evernote, which limits access on its free plan, Notion lets you use multiple devices, even on its free tier.
Credit: Nathaniel Pangaro / How-To Geek | Samsung
 This is especially useful for features that may not be available on the mobile version but are accessible on the desktop version. It also allows me to switch between devices easily—for example, writing on my phone while I’m out and continuing on my laptop when I get home.

            A new note-taking journey begins
    
Evernote used to be a great free option, but it has become so limited that it’s no longer practical for daily use. With 
Notion
, I’ve been able to enjoy journaling again without worrying about limits or constant upgrade prompts.
It was time to say goodbye to Evernote and move on, and by 
switching to Notion
, I am not only happy with my decision but also not looking back anytime soon.
