If you're perpetually online like me, it's likely that you keep running into the same issue. You'll read something useful, forget about the tab, eventually close it, and lose it. By the time it's the weekend, and you're thinking about that long read you were saving up, the tab is long gone, and you start the process of finding it through a series of unwieldy searches.
✕
 Remove Ads
Let's be real, 
in-browser history
 doesn't really help all that much. It's better than nothing, but the entire concept is built around URLs and titles with little to no context, and if you're diving through ten of them, you'll have to open all ten to see if that's what you were looking at. If you, for example, can only remember a sentence or an idea instead of the exact page, browser history is of no use.
So, in an attempt to solve the problem, I set up Hister. This is a self-hosted 
search engine
 that indexes the pages I visit and makes them searchable locally. It's not a replacement for a search engine for discovery. Instead, it gives you a locally cached copy of everything you've visited that you can search in natural language and, with keywords, solves a very specific problem — remembering what I've already looked at.

                        Discovery-based search solves the wrong problem
               

            Turn your browsing history into something searchable
    
 Contrary to what I used to believe, most of my searches aren't for identifying fresh information. All too often, I'm searching for the same content again. I might've read something on my phone, and when I get back home, I search for the same information. It's a very different problem, but we still use the same tools for it.
✕
 Remove Ads
Search engines prioritize freshness, authority, and popularity, but they don't really know what I've seen before or what I found useful. Browser history attempts to fill that gap, but it doesn't. It's a little more than a log. Hister approaches this problem differently. This 
self-hosted app
 builds a local index of the pages I visit, including the actual text on those pages. This means I can search based on the information I remember instead of having to remember URLs or titles.
That's a big difference in approach when it comes to surfacing information. Using Hister allows me to type up fragments of sentences or a rough idea and still find the right page. It's not beholden to ever-evolving ranking, or SEO patterns designed for the broader web. Plus, it also captures things that standard search wouldn't. Since Hister is constantly recording whatever I'm searching for, it can help me locate pages tucked away behind logins, internal dashboards, and more.

                        Built to fit into every browsing workflow
               

            Easy setup and full control over your data
    
✕
 Remove Ads
Setting up Hister is easy thanks to the well-detailed documentation. You just need to install the server component and the accompanying browser extension, and you're good to go. Hister will start caching all your browsing from then on. However, it gets even better. You can import your entire existing 
browser
 history into Hister. While it won't have the same depth of text-based caching that you get with active browsing, it still tends to be a big improvement over your usual history-based search. All of which combined means that you can very well set up Hister as your default search engine, as I have.
The most common use case is simple recall. If I remember points from a feature I read a week back, I can start typing out those keywords naturally. Since they are bound to be found within the copy of the article, Hister starts pulling up related articles in real time. From there its a simple matter of dipping into the specific article. Because it indexes the full content of the page, you can browse the slimmed-down 
cache
 right on the side, or tap to open the original article. In addition to the convenience of finding your old articles, there's a big difference in speed. Since everything runs locally, results appear instantly. There is no dependency on external services or tracking.
I get it, you might not want Hister to index everything. Be it private documents or banking information, not everything needs to be searchable. There are ample rules-based controls built in to help you control the flow. You can choose to exclude sites, tweak the behavior, and shape the index the way that works best for your needs. Plus, since the entire index is cached on your computer, it is not just private by default; it is also available entirely offline. That's a big advantage if you find yourself in low or no-network regions often enough, but it also protects against pages being pulled off the internet.

And finally, before you ask, yes, you can use Hister as the launch pad to dive into traditional search engines as well, making it the perfect default search engine for your browser.

            The fastest way to find what you've already read
    
Hister is one of those few services that can truly elevate how you interact with the internet. It turns your browsing history into something easily searchable and useful, and helps you surface the information that you've already consumed.
No, it doesn't replace traditional 
search engines
. Instead, it makes you more efficient at search by reducing how often you need to go down a path of discovery. And at its most basic level, it solves the very simple problem of finding something you know you've already read before.
