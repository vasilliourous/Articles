How To Stop Feature Creep And Prioritize Product Value
By
Expert Panel®
,
Forbes Councils Member.
for 
Forbes Technology Council
COUNCIL POST
Expertise from Forbes Councils members, operated under license. Opinions expressed are those of the author.
 | Membership (fee-based)
Apr 16, 2026, 01:15pm EDT
getty
Feature creep has a way of sneaking into product organizations. What starts as a well-scoped roadmap can turn into a crowded mix of nice-to-haves, legacy add-ons and stakeholder-driven requests that are hard to challenge, especially when every feature has an internal champion. Deciding what to build, improve on or retire becomes a test of discipline, alignment and a team’s willingness to let evidence lead.
The smartest strategies look beyond raw adoption and consider the fuller picture of customer value, business impact, strategic fit and the ongoing cost of complexity. Below, members of 
Forbes Technology Council
 discuss helpful criteria and frameworks product leaders can use to optimize feature build and sunset decisions.
Audit Features Each Quarter
Run a quarterly feature audit, listing all product features. Then, score each feature based on usage, performance, importance and the effort needed to support or operationalize it. Identify the features that do not meet the majority of the criteria above or that perform under a certain metric. Decide on the next action, which could be to improve, merge or sunset the feature. -
 Sarvesh Nispat Desai
, KPMG LLP
PROMOTED
Filter Features Through Mission And Need
Use a mission and impact filter: If a feature doesn’t serve your core purpose or a meaningful customer need, it’s time to reconsider it. Next, evaluate redundancy. Your role isn’t to build everything but to enable a flexible, connected tech stack that avoids silos and empowers the customer. The best software knows its purpose and executes it well. -
 Vanessa Chambers
,
 Martus Solutions
Forbes Technology Council
 is an invitation-only community for world-class CIOs, CTOs and technology executives. 
Do I qualify?
Ask Whether It’s A Feature Or A Product
“Is this a feature or a product?” can be a powerful question to ask. Not only does it force you to define what your product actually is, but it also forces you to admit when a product has grown beyond the scope. Once you have defined which features are part of a separate product, the question becomes whether you want to continue developing the second product or find someone else to focus on it. -
 Kevin Korte
,
 Univention
Score Features With The FRAME Framework
I use a four-pillar framework called FRAME: I look at feature usage, revenue impact, audience value and maintenance load. Each feature is rated across these dimensions. If a feature has low usage and limited customer and revenue value, but high support and maintenance overhead, it is a strong candidate for sunsetting. This is a simple way to make feature retirement decisions more evidence-based and less emotional. -
 Sandeep Shivam
,
 Tavant
Weigh Usage Against Maintenance Cost
Track usage versus maintenance cost, but avoid the trap of all-or-nothing decisions. Ask, “Does this feature serve all customers or just top- or mid-tier customers, or is it a one-off?” With development costs dropping, the real question is where a feature creates the most leverage. Features that don’t scale across segments belong in a paid tier or should be sunsetted, not part of the core roadmap. -
 Salim Gheewalla
,
 utilITise

Revisit Customer Needs 
This is a three-step process: First, list the customer needs this feature is intended to meet. Second, ask customers how well those needs are being met. Third, if alternatives are better positioned to serve customer needs, sunset the feature (unless it has strategic value as part of a bundle). -
 Rajiv Gupta
,
 Amazon Web Services
Use A Value-Versus-Cost Matrix
The balance between the value and the associated cost of a feature (including maintenance) can be a good framework for making decisions. A low-value, high-cost feature can be a good candidate to say “no” to. Once you’ve gone through the cost versus value matrix, it’s important to measure each feature’s impact via adoption and analytics to make eventual decisions on sunsets. -
 Abhinav Sinha
,
 ZentrumHub
Limit Active Priorities To Three
At any given time, restrict active projects to the top three. If there is a new idea or feature to work on, the team should make a justified and deliberate decision that the new feature will replace one of the existing three on the list—not add to the list. -
 Boyan Ivanov
,
 StorPool Storage
Track A Feature Value Decay Index
Adopt a feature value decay index: Track each feature’s ratio of active usage frequency against its maintenance cost over rolling quarters. When the index falls below a defined threshold, it triggers a structured sunset review. This depoliticizes “no” by replacing opinion with evidence, shifting conversations from ownership pride to portfolio optimization tied to measurable ROI and engineering velocity. -
 Rishi Gupta
,
 Infosys DX Consulting
Measure Usage Per Cost
Use “usage per cost” as a clear signal. If a feature has low active use but high maintenance costs, it’s a candidate to sunset. Pair this with a simple rule: If fewer than X% of users rely on it and it doesn’t drive revenue or retention, it should be retired. -
 Lori Schafer
,
 Digital Wave Technology
Track Support Tickets By Feature
Forget usage dashboards. Track support tickets per feature instead. Microsoft’s animated digital assistant, Clippy, would have shown high engagement by traditional metrics, yet
 Microsoft killed it
 because it created more confusion than value. I call this the “drag coefficient.” If a feature creates more friction than value, sunset it. Customer pain is harder to argue with than usage charts. -
 Ankit Narayan Singh
,
 ParallelDots, Inc.
Compare Adoption With Upkeep
Track feature adoption rates alongside maintenance costs. If a feature has low usage but high upkeep, the data speaks for itself, removing politics from the conversation. A simple value-versus-cost matrix gives product leaders an objective basis to sunset features without it feeling like a personal or political call. -
 Ajit Sahu
,
 Data SafeGuard INC.
Apply The RICE Framework
With feature-heavy products, saying “no” gets easier with the RICE framework: reach, impact, confidence, effort. It turns tough debates into clear math. If a feature reaches few users, delivers little impact and demands high effort, the decision becomes obvious: Sunset it. Data replaces politics, helping teams focus energy on what truly moves the product forward. -
 Vishwanadham Mandala
, 
Cummins Inc.
Use A Feature Removal ‘Regret Test’
Use a “regret test.” Ask, “If we removed this feature, who would truly fight to get it back, and what revenue, retention or workflow would be disrupted?” Many features survive politics because no one measures the pain of keeping them versus the pain of losing them. -
 Margarita Simonova
,
 ILoveMyQA
Calculate The ‘Cost Of Carry’
Every feature has a maintenance tax: engineering time, test coverage, edge cases in every release. Most product organizations track adoption but never tally what keeping a low-usage feature actually costs over 12 months. When you put that number next to a 3% usage rate, the conversation changes. “No” stops being political and starts being math. -
 Ankit Agrawal
,
 Equifax
Score Feature Requests By Business Impact
Rate every new feature request across four dimensions: revenue generated, loss prevented, retention improved and reputation preserved, in that order. If a feature scores low across all four, it doesn’t make the cut. This framework depoliticizes prioritization—you’re not saying “no” to someone’s idea; you’re letting the numbers decide. -
 Aruna Veerappan
Map Features On A Usage-Sentiment Quadrant
Deploy a usage versus sentiment quadrant to make surgical cuts. High-sentiment but low-usage features are often “vanity tools” that drain budgets without driving scale. By visualizing this gap, you provide a shield against stakeholders. It’s said in retail that “less is often more when it creates a frictionless journey.” Use this data to justify a leaner, more intentional product roadmap for the team. -
 Uttam Kumar
,
 American Eagle Outfitters
Set A ‘Last 90 Days’ Rule
Use the “last 90 days” rule: If a feature hasn’t been voluntarily used, without prompting, by a meaningful threshold of users—say, 10%—in the last 90 days, it enters automatic sunset review. This creates a standing policy, not a one-off judgment call, so no single leader “owns” the kill decision—neutralizing the politics entirely. -
 Michael Flickinger
,
 Bizowie
Prioritize Features Through Strategic Alignment
Use a strategy-first prioritization framework. Assess each feature against customer impact, adoption and alignment to your long-term product vision. Prioritization isn’t about cutting features—it’s about protecting focus. Say “no” to distractions, but stay flexible when opportunities accelerate your strategic roadmap and deepen core value. -
 Divye Khilnani
,
 Intuit Inc.
Take A ‘Spring Cleaning’ Approach
In my experience, teams often become internally attached to features they believe will make a difference, even when user feedback suggests otherwise. A useful reference is Google’s “Spring Cleaning” approach, where products and features are regularly reviewed based on user adoption, strategic relevance and maintenance cost, and those that no longer deliver clear value are sunset. -
 Michael Tyrimos
,
 Capacitor Partners
