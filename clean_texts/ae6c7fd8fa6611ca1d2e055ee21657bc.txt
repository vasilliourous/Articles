Besides saving you a couple of dollars every month in subscription fees, the privacy-respecting nature is the biggest advantage of FOSS tools. Since everything runs on your local machine, you don’t have to worry about the firms harvesting your data and using it to train their AI models. You also don’t have to put up with them selling your private data to even larger corporations or deal with paid ads everywhere.

In fact, most services in my application stack are meant to be replacements for conventional cloud platforms. But if I had to pinpoint specific services that helped me migrate from privacy-intrusive applications, it'd be these six tools.

                        Vert
               

            Bonus points if you pair it with Vertd
    
If you’ve been working with computers for a while, you’ve probably had a situation where you needed to convert a specific file into different formats. Unfortunately, between their file size (and number) limits, sign-up requirements, and long queue times, online file conversion tools are far from user-friendly. That’s before you include the privacy problems of letting external platforms access the original and converted files. 
Vert gets rid of these issues in one fell swoop by letting you convert typical documents, images, or audio files on your local machine. Besides running multiple conversion tasks in tandem, 
Vert
 is also fairly quick at transforming your files into different formats, even if you deploy it on a modest server. It supports pretty much every audio, image, and document extension you could ever need, though some of the obscure ones can only be accepted as input files. For folks who’ve got a somewhat beefy machine with a decent GPU, you can even self-host Vertd, an FFmpeg wrapper that adds video conversion provisions to Vert’s neat web UI.

                        BentoPDF 
               

            It’s a lot faster than cloud PDF editors
    
Just like their file conversion counterparts, cloud-based PDF editors have similar quality-of-life problems, on top of bearing the extra drawback of often charging a subscription fee just to remove their watermarks. And if you work with press releases, academic documents, confidential benchmark sheets, and other non-disclosure PDFs as I do, you’ll have to remove cloud platforms from the equation altogether.
Fortunately, 
BentoPDF
 provides every document editing utility you can ask for, all while running on your own hardware. Whether it’s annotating PDFs, splitting them into individual pages, modifying their metadata, or simply changing their contents, BentoPDF can pull off these tasks without stressing your machine or testing your patience.

                        Vaultwarden 
               

            It's pretty safe to use, too
    
✕
 Remove Ads
Considering that you’ll have to manage everything yourself, the prospect of leaving the heavy lifting to a cloud-based password manager sounds pretty neat. But if you’re as paranoid about housing your credentials on a random company’s servers, the self-hosted Vaultwarden can be a solid alternative. It’s a lot lighter on the resource consumption front than Bitwarden, while possessing all the password management features you can ask for.
Besides passwords, 
Vaultwarden
 can store TOTP codes, passkeys, API keys, bank card details, and even identify documents. It’s also pretty handy if you want to generate random strings with specific alphanumeric parameters. Since you can pair it with Bitwarden’s bookmark manager and client apps, you don’t have to look around for the web browser every time you need to access your passwords. On paper, there’s always the problem of losing your credentials to a broken Vaultwarden instance. But since most client apps you can pair with Vaultwarden retain a copy of your passwords and allow you to extract their databases, you don’t need to get anxious about getting digitally marooned if your Vaultwarden container becomes temporarily inoperable.

                        Syncthing 
               

            But Rsync and Kopia are better for backups
    
✕
 Remove Ads
There are plenty of tools for syncing files between multiple devices, but 
Syncthing
 gets my vote as the best one in the self-hosted ecosystem. It’s available on practically every major operating system, including the likes of FreeBSD and Illumos, and you can fine-tune different sync settings to ensure you don’t accidentally overwrite files when syncing files across multiple devices. 
Personally, I rely on Syncthing to ensure my coding files are accessible across my MacBook, gaming PC, and dev virtual machine. That way, I can continue where I left off once I switch to a different system. Technically, you can configure 1-way syncs to a central storage server as a makeshift backup arrangement, but Syncthing isn’t ideal for creating redundant datasets. Instead, I’d recommend looking into Rsync (or Kopia, for a simpler UI) if you want a backup utility instead of a data synchronization tool.

                        SearXNG 
               

            To keep privacy-intrusive search engines at bay
    
If you don’t want search engines to track your personal information, self-hosting a SearXNG instance can be a solid alternative. Since it’s a metasearch engine, SearXNG aggregates the results from different search engines, and you’re free to choose the ones it uses to index the web. Besides sparing you from an ad-riddled mess of search results, SearXNG is a great way to mitigate the profiling antics carried out by typical search engines.
✕
 Remove Ads
The best part? SearXNG is compatible with Open WebUI, and once you pair these services together, you can use local LLMs to pull results from the Internet when responding to your queries for a completely FOSS workflow.

                        Home Assistant 
               

            A central hub for your smart home
    
Despite making your life more convenient, smart devices that connect to the cloud are riddled with privacy and security problems. Leaving aside the fact that their outdated firmware, weak encryption, and easy-to-guess passwords make them vulnerable to man-in-the-middle attacks, hooking your security cameras and devices with presence detection sensors to external servers is a terrible idea from a privacy standpoint. 
Home Assistant not only solves these privacy issues, but it also provides a centralized interface to manage your smart gizmos and IoT paraphernalia. This way, you don’t have to cycle between different apps if you’re using devices from different manufacturers. Home Assistant also has killer automation provisions that, when combined with its compatibility with different smart devices and self-hosted services, let you design practical (and wacky) trigger-action chains involving multiple entities.

            Some more suggestions for privacy-conscious folks
    
With all the FOSS tools out there, this list is far from complete. I initially wanted to mention Nextcloud (and the integrations available in its app store) in the list, but that’s usually the first thing that comes to mind in an article about privacy-respecting services. So, I wanted to give it a shout-out here. Likewise, Jellyfin is an amazing tool for managing movies ripped from physical media, while RomM performs a similar role for ROM files. Immich is a great alternative to Google Photos, though I’d recommend setting up a remote backup server if you want to use it to safeguard your photo library. 
Speaking of managing media files, I’ve got a self-hosted Firefly III instance keeping track of my financial transactions, with a Paperless-ngx setup (involving extra companions apps) in charge of housing all my documents. If we’re going by technical terms, Cryptomator isn’t quite a self-hosted app you’d run inside a container and access from a web UI, but it’s a godsend for folks who want client-side encryption to prevent cloud platforms from snooping into their private documents.
