I rebuilt my home server after 3 years, and these mistakes nearly cost me everything

As someone who has been a part of the home lab ecosystem for almost a decade, I’ve cycled between several devices over the years. Heck, I’ve switched distributions, container runtimes, and virtualization platforms a bunch of times before I ended up with my current software arsenal. However, one thing that has remained consistent all this time is the amount of experience I gained from my tinkering misadventures and botched experiments.
✕

In fact, I still remember some of the fatal mistakes I made when I switched to my current Xeon workstation after using my old Ryzen build for almost three years. While I’ve come a long way since then, here’s a quick overview of everything I did wrong when I rebuilt my primary home server, so you don’t repeat the same mistakes as my amateur self.

                        Forgetting to tweak the virtual guests I’d armed with GPUs
               

            Or even the CPU host setting, for that matter
    
I’ve always loved being able to pass PCI devices to my virtual guests. So much so that I’d configured GPU passthrough for a bunch of LXCs on my Ryzen-based Proxmox node, and even set up provisions to quickly wipe out the config files and move my graphics card to my virtual machines if I wanted to work on demanding projects.
Well, what I failed to realize was that my virtual guests wouldn’t work unless I modified their configs and removed the old GPU drivers to accommodate my Team Blue (specifically the Intel Arc A750) card. For reference, I had a Proxmox Backup Server in charge of housing the snapshots, but never quite realized I could use it to migrate my virtual guests. Instead, I used the ol’ method of creating vzdump backups, moving them to my PC, and using an SCP tool to migrate my LXCs and VMs over to the other node (which is one heck of a dumb move on its own).
✕
 Remove Ads
As you’d expect, attempting to boot my virtual guests either resulted in them stopping immediately or running into driver issues every once in a while. Worse still, I’d realized I could slap the host CPU setting on every VM to boost its performance, which further complicated things post-migration. Luckily, I had ample documentation to realize my mistake and make things right with the CPU setting, though the GPU passthrough problem was only resolved after I uninstalled the drivers on the affected LXCs (and in some cases, after spinning up new ones).

                        Clustering everything on a 2-node setup… without a Q-Device
               

            In hindsight, I didn’t even need a cluster back then
    
Building clusters when you don’t really require them is something I always caution new home labbers from doing, especially since I speak from experience. You see, once I’d finished moving the virtual guests and gotten accustomed to the Xeon node, I still had my old PC in a functioning state. So, I did what any tinkerer would do in this situation – continue using it for my server experiments. And once I’d stumbled upon the cluster provisions on Proxmox, I figured I could configure them on my 2-node home lab.
✕
 Remove Ads
Now, there’s nothing wrong with a cluster comprising only two nodes. Heck, my current PVE cluster (which coincidentally uses the same Ryzen system) includes two systems in a 
ZFS replication-powered high-availability setup
. But I have a third Raspberry Pi acting as the Quorum Device, meaning my cluster will still remain online even if either device goes offline. Without my pint-sized companion, my cluster would lose the tie-breaker device to maintain quorum if either node goes down. That’s pretty much what happened a few weeks later after I’d deployed my first two-node cluster. Since I wanted to check what 
XCP-ng
 was all about, I removed the Proxmox drive on my Ryzen system and began installing the platform, blissfully unaware that my main PVE node had also gone down. Fortunately, I’d only swapped the SSD on the former; otherwise, I would’ve had to reset everything and spend hours restoring snapshots from my PBS server.

                        Overprovisioning the system resources
               

            Some habits die hard
    
Truth be told, this isn’t something I realized immediately after I upgraded my server, as I was too busy enjoying the extra computational resources at my disposal thanks to the ex-enterprise rig. For starters, I began allocating RAM to my virtual guests with reckless abandon, and would often run GUI-heavy VMs even for projects that didn’t need them. Over time, I’d overprovisioned my memory resources so much that my system had started to feel sluggish, a situation that wasn’t helped by the fact that I was using ZFS pools on my storage server. Once I ended up crashing the server, I realized my folly and began optimizing my resource allocation habits, and even started moving away from intensive VMs to lightweight LXCs.

That wasn’t the worst part, though. Besides assigning too much RAM to my virtual guests, I’d also gone overboard when giving them virtual SCSI disks, to the point where my VMs and LXCs occupied over twice the max storage capacity of my server. The thing is, they didn’t end up consuming all that space, so my local-lvm and ZFS pools reported a lot of free space. But I didn’t realize this mistake until after I tried upgrading to 
PVE 9 last year
. After successfully updating to the new Proxmox version, my node threw an error mentioning my storage pools were offline. After a lot of panicked searches, I came across a forum post highlighting the same problem, and running the commands a kind tinkerer had mentioned in the thread helped resolve this issue. But it taught me about the importance of keeping my resource allocation tendencies under control.

            Home labbing is all about trial and error, anyway
    
Although I can’t stop smirking while I type this article, I’d say it’s worth documenting my mistakes. If not for newcomers, then at least for myself. Every issue I’ve encountered so far has made me a little more proficient in the fine art of home labbing. And that’s pretty much what home servers are meant for – making new mistakes frequently and learning valuable lessons... occasionally.
