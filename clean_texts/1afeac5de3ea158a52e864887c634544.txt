In a rational world, Linux is just an operating system and people who genuinely believe it's a better operating system than the alternatives would want it to become as easy to use and popular as possible.
Yet if you spend time among the most hardcore Linux faithful on their subreddits or other similar forums, there's always a sense of derision. The idea that Linux shouldn't be as easy to use as Windows or macOS. That people who want to use Linux should "
learn how computers work
" or other similar soundbites that pepper the usual discourse.

This is revealing to me, because I've seen it play out in so many other subcultures over the years. It shows that the core group of Linux fans consider its obscurity and learning curve a feature rather than a bug.

                        Ease of use threatens the “in-group”
               

            You need to go through the tribal hazing before you're accepted
    
Across the history of humankind, there has always been some sort of barrier to entry when someone wants to join a group. For some tribal societies, you're only considered an adult if you pass some sort of test. Consider the Sateré-Mawé tribe of the Brazilian Amazon, who have a coming-of-age ritual where 
young men wear gloves filled with bullet ants
.
Endure the incredibly-painful bites without showing pain and you're part of the club!
Hazing in fraternities and sororities is just another version of this, and it's common to all exclusive in-groups in some way. The curious thing is that if someone were to suggest getting rid of that barrier, the value of joining the groups goes down. Belonging to the group matters because it's hard. It doesn't matter whether the friction is unnecessary.
So, basically, I'm saying that using the Linux terminal is like shoving your hands into gloves full of Bullet Ants, and Linux fans like it that way.
If you think that's a stretch, here's an excellent video by 
bigboxSWE
 that neatly summarizes the bitter taste of the extreme end of the Linux subculture.
That rabbit hole goes as deep as you dare, though I don't recommend you dare go any further. Instead, let's turn to the consequences.

                        Gatekeeping is disguised as standards
               

            Let the right one in
    
Credit: Air Images/Shutterstock.com
 This desire to keep the riffraff out can manifest in a few ways, but a common tactic I've observed is to say that new features such as 
Flatpak
 or distros like Ubuntu and 
Zorin OS
 aren't "real" parts of the Linux experience, and by extension, the people who use them aren't real Linux users.
Only the Linux that preserves the knowledge moat around these hardcore fans counts, and any attempt to bridge that moat is seen as an attack on the "purity" of Linux.

                        Mainstream success would erase the subculture
               

            Won't anyone think of the geeks?
    
If desktop Linux went mainstream today, it would pretty much eradicate this elite Linux subculture. There's certainly no true equivalent on the Windows side of the fence. Sure, there is such a thing as a Windows "power user" who knows the OS inside and out, but there's nothing like the Linux subculture I'm referring to here.
You see the same thing happens with other fandoms that break out into the mainstream. It happened with anime, it happens with indie bands that make it big who are then accused by early supporters of being "sellouts."

People attach their own unique identities to an external thing, form a cohesive group, and then (consciously or not) fight tooth and nail to preserve that identity.

The thing is, the person who wants to switch to Linux because they don't like what Windows has become isn't looking to become part of a subculture. They want a computer that works without the compromises of Windows. They don't want to spend time learning terminal commands instead of working or playing. The computer is a tool. It's a means to an end, not the end-goal itself.

            The niche would simply move elsewhere
    
The irony of it all is that if Linux became as popular, easy, and polished as macOS or Windows today, these same people would just move into something else. Maybe it would be 
FreeBSD
, or 
HaikuOS
, or something truly off the wall like 
Plan 9 from Bell Labs
.
They could even create new, harsher Linux moats against the normies. Projects like 
Linux From Scratch
 or ultra-minimal setups (think Gentoo or custom Arch builds stripped to the bone) create a new barrier to entry inside the same ecosystem. As long as the line can be drawn between "us" and "them" it doesn't matter whether the actual software is any good or not.

This article mentions a real problem that lots of people in the Linux community have noticed. Unfortunately, the article flops a bit because the author has not thought through what any of the solutions would entail, nor is this mentioned at any point.
It's easy to say "wouldn't Linux be more awesome if it was easier to use like windows or macOS?". Ok, sure. Nobody whos rational and not gatekeeping would argue with that. But that's not a solution to anything, that's a vague ideal we would like to move towards. Got any suggestions as to how?
This topic isn't exactly new, I've seen various forms of this take for years on end ever since I got into Linux. But unless the article has some tangible suggestions as to what we could do to make Linux easier while still being Linux and not just a macOS knockoff, it comes across as a bunch of hot air.
The problem is I suspect the author has not done a ton of research into what that would entail. Because it's a hard problem. Even skilled programmers and UI designers are not in full agreement about the best way to handle it. It absolutely cannot come at the cost of making anything in the OS less scriptable/programmable because the entire Linux philosophy revolves around being able to modify the system using code and not needing to use GUIs for system configuration. No matter what is added or how much easier it becomes, that element is sacred and Linux stops being Linux if that goes away. But anyway, the only reason I'm mentioning this is to illustrate that the solution is not simple and you should be skeptical of anyone that doesn't know Linux well enough to understand the programmable aspect of the OS is core to the philosophy and additions must work alongside that instead of replacing it. That's a hard design problem.
