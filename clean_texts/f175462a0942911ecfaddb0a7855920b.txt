If you’ve been a part of the home lab ecosystem for a long time, you’ll often hear veterans complaining about the hassle of managing multiple virtual guests, detailed backup pipelines, network stacks, and clusters. I agree with them to some extent, as there comes a point in this hobby when everything starts to feel like a second job.

That said, it's only a matter of time before you get bitten by the home labbing bug and start building an overkill setup. Sure, most consumers don’t need to go this far, but few things are as satisfying as building a secure, automated, and over-engineered high-availability setup.

                        My high-availability clusters and backup workflows protect essential services 
               

            A reliable error recovery pipeline is better than worrying about botched experiments
    
When you’re working in a home lab environment, things can and will go wrong. That’s perfectly fine as a beginner, when you’re playing with cool FOSS utilities and quirky distributions. But once you’ve got a reliable collection of containerized apps and virtual machines for everyday tasks, building entire servers from scratch when projects go south would be a massive pain. And trust me, all it takes is one incorrect variable in the wrong config file to break the entire setup. Yeah, I speak from experience.
But instead of shying away from unsafe experiments, I’ve bolstered my data recovery provisions. For example, my Proxmox virtual guests get backed up to my local PBS instance almost every other day (though my low-priority LXCs and VMs have a longer frequency between each snapshot task). I’ve also configured automated sync tasks to save essential snapshots to an external Proxmox Backup Server (one that runs as a VM on a remote NAS), and the frequency of these operations varies between once every fortnight and once every month. The same applies to the important datasets on my NAS (except, both TrueNAS instances are bare-metal).

I’ve also got a 2-node high-availability PVE cluster involving ZFS replication between cheap x86 devices, with a Raspberry Pi serving as the Q-device, and I use it to separate my essential LXCs and virtual machines from my primary workstation. You know, stuff like my Pi-hole instance, documentation-heavy 
Trilium Notes
 container, 
Pulse
 dashboard, 
Gotify
 server, and 
Ollama LXC
. That way, I can limit the damage caused by a botched project running on my main 
Xeon server
. It’s definitely overkill, but the extra measures make my DevOps experiments a lot more stress-free. 

                        I consider my excessive security precautions vital for my home lab 
               

            But I’ll admit that they’re a pain to troubleshoot
    
If we’re talking about things I find somewhat annoying to maintain, I have to point to the security services in my app stack. I’ve got an OPNsense router at the center of my networking stack, and I’ve armed it with strict firewall rules (well, mostly deny all as the default, with exceptions configured for specific devices – virtual or otherwise). I’ve also tossed some VLANs that work in tandem with my managed switch, and this is especially important because it safeguards everything else on my LAN from the smart devices I’ve scattered across my living space. I’ve also started configuring SSO logins via 
Authentik
 for the services I’ve shared with my family members, though I still have a lot of work left on this front. 
✕
 Remove Ads
Had I stuck to simple firewall rules on Windows, I would’ve never learned anything about reverse proxies, SSL certifications, NAT, ACME clients, and other networking jargon.
Yeah, controlling my network stack can be a pain, but modifying security rules and tinkering with cool (yet dangerous) utilities can be quite thrilling, especially once everything starts falling into place.

                        Automating my home lab is just as much fun 
               

            Especially workflows involving multiple devices and virtual guests
    
I’d say that my automation services are far more optional than dedicated backups and security tools, but being able to trigger long workflow chains using simple events is a lot of fun. Take my 
Semaphore server
, for example. Besides arming it with 
Terraform
 and 
Ansible
, I’ve hooked it up to my main Proxmox machine, just so I could spin up disposable virtual guests. 
I’ve been using it to train my DevOps skills, and I daresay that misconfigured Playbooks and broken pipelines have taught me more than any book or lecture during my university days. Likewise, I’ve been dabbling in n8n workflows, and once you get the ball rolling, it’s hard to stop. The same applies to Node-RED, especially for smart home automations spanning a handful of entities. 

            Above all else, home labbing is about learning and having fun
    
✕
 Remove Ads
Although I rely on my servers for their FOSS goodies, my initial motivations for this hobby were a lot simpler – I was looking for a safe environment where I could test anything that looked interesting. And that still hasn’t changed after all this time. I may have over-engineered my setup, but I still have as much fun messing around with wacky distributions and unhinged container images.
