I have been in the homelabbing game for over five years at this point, and I have tried out, quite literally, hundreds of services to self-host. These days, I have around 30 to 40 services running in my homelab at any given time, but these five are the ones I can’t live without.
✕

                        Plex
               

            Running my own Netflix is just too addicting
    
Credit: Lucas Gouveia/How-To Geek | ZinetroN/
Shutterstock
Plex
 was my entry to the homelab world, and it’s still going strong. While my actual homelab might only be five years old, I’ve been running a Plex server for closer to 15 years at this point.
Running Plex in my homelab
 allowed me to completely ditch streaming media providers. I used to have an extensive DVD and Blu-ray collection—when a new Marvel or other similar type of movie would come out, I’d instantly pre-order the 4K Blu-ray Steelbook version of it. I was an avid collector, but 
I hated putting the disc into a player to watch
.
While most movies came with digital copies, they were far too limited for my liking, so I eventually started ripping movies to my computer and I spun up a Plex server to watch them everywhere. The rest, as it they say, was history.
✕
 Remove Ads
Plex is the lifeblood of my content consumption now, and I simply couldn’t live without running Plex at this point. It’s too convenient, the service is reliable, and it works everywhere I go, all with data sitting on a server in my home office.

                        Audiobookshelf
               

            Being able to stream my own audiobooks is so freeing
    
 Last year I really got into listening to audiobooks, and I plan to continue that this year. Audible is simply too expensive and too limiting for me, though. That’s why 
I ditched Audible for Audiobookshelf
.
There’s a thrift store my wife and I like to go to where they have lots of audiobooks for sale on CD, which helps to expand my audiobook library. I’m also able to purchase audiobooks directly from the source now, instead of having to go through Audible. Plus, it’s easier for me to share the same audiobook library with my entire household where we each have our own progress on audiobooks.
✕
 Remove Ads
Audiobookshelf
 is basically Plex for audiobooks. I know that Plex has native audiobook support, and I used Plex for audiobooks for quite a while, but Audiobookshelf is next level when it comes to how it handles things.
At this point, I rely more on Audiobookshelf then many other services in my homelab. It’s my wife’s most used service in the homelab, for sure.

                        Home Assistant
               

            Everything is HomeKit compatible when Home Assistant is involved
    
Home Assistant
 is the lifeblood of my smart home. I’m a HomeKit user through and through, but not every device in my smart home is HomeKit compatible.
Early on, I tried to make sure everything I bought worked natively with HomeKit. That’s a noble desire, but a fairly difficult one to achieve, I found out. I did pretty good at it for the most part, until my wife and I were at a yard sale one day and I found some Gosund Wi-Fi smart plugs for $1 each, still new in the box.
✕
 Remove Ads
These plugs worked with Alexa and Google Assistant, but not HomeKit—at least, not natively. That was the push I needed to 
finally adopt Home Assistant
 into my life, and now I couldn’t live without it.
For me, Home Assistant is purely 
a way to bridge my HomeKit setup with non-Homekit devices
. I know that Home Assistant is way more powerful than just acting as a HomeKit bridge, and I do plan to utilize it in a greater capacity in the future, but it works perfectly for what I need right now.

                        Scrypted
               

            HomeKit Secure Video for non-HomeKit cameras
    
Credit: Patrick Campanale / How-To Geek
 Just like Home Assistant, 
Scrypted
 is the way that 
I get non-HomeKit cameras into HomeKit
, because HomeKit is life for me. For a while, 
I was using Scrypted as an NVR
 in my homelab, but eventually moved recording to 
HomeKit Secure Video
 because it’s simpler to maintain and included with my iCloud+ subscription.
✕
 Remove Ads
These days, Scrypted is very much a background app for me, but it’s absolutely crucial to my homelab experience. I have a handful of non-HomeKit Secure Video cameras, and I integrate them all into Scrypted, which then transports them to HomeKit Secure Video.
It’s a pretty simple setup all around, but it’s one that I couldn’t live without in my homelab at this point.

                        Pi-hole
               

            Content filtering and custom DNS done in-house
    
Credit: Patrick Campanale / How-To Geek
 I honestly had no idea how many calls the devices in my homelab made until I deployed 
Pi-hole
. Pi-hole is best known for ad-blocking—and it’s great for that—but it also does so much more.
A few years ago, I tried to sign up for the 
Auvik trial
 to get a free network switch. I deployed their collector to a few devices on my network, ended up not qualifying, and thought I had removed everything entirely. It turns out, there was one device on the network that it didn’t fully get removed from, and it was constantly calling home to Auvik.
✕
 Remove Ads

With Pi-hole, I was able to see this activity, track it down to the device that it was happening on, and properly uninstall Auvik from it. I’ve also been able to block other things on the network, like some smart devices that phone home to China but I use Home Assistant to control. They don’t need to call home to China anymore since 
I use local control
, and Pi-hole allows me to block those calls easily.
I’m also able to have custom DNS records in my homelab. Think 
proxmox.domain.com
 or anything else like that, but without having to register a domain or 
have the service publicly available
.
Pi-hole is extremely useful in my homelab for far more than just ad blocking, and I couldn’t imagine not having it deployed in my homelab.

            My homelab is constantly changing, but these services will never leave
    
I might be deploying, testing, and removing services from my homelab constantly, but I have no plans on sunsetting any of these services whatsoever. Plex is a staple for me, and though Jellyfin is more private and open source, 
I just don’t see myself leaving Plex anytime soon
.
✕
 Remove Ads
Audiobookshelf is absolutely a must for me, and both Home Assistant and Scrypted expand my Apple ecosystem far further than Apple ever will. Pi-hole is just a simple piece of software that just works, and I see zero reason to replace it.
Your homelab will eventually grow to the point where you have services like these—ones you rely on and have no plans of replacing. It’s a great feeling to know that in a homelab where so much changes so often, there’s still a bit of consistency at the end of the day.
