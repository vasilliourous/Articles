                        Wi-Fi may be faster than Ethernet on your TV
               

            The rare case where Wi-Fi wins
    
Pankil Shah / MakeUseOf
Credit: Pankil Shah / MakeUseOf
 It might sound wrong at first. For years, Ethernet has been the gold standard for speed and reliability. Plug it in, and you get a consistent performance without any interference.
But the thing is, most smart TVs come with a built-in Ethernet port that tops out at just 100 Mbps. Not gigabit. Not even close. Meanwhile, modern TVs do come with 
Wi-Fi 5 or Wi-Fi 6
. Under the right conditions, that wireless connection can easily push past 100Mbps, sometimes much higher depending on your internet plan.
The reason manufacturers stick with a slower Ethernet port on a smart TV mostly comes down to cost and practicality. You see, smart TVs rarely need ultra-fast wired internet for what they do. Streaming services like Netflix, YouTube, and Prime Video don’t require anywhere near 100 Mbps, even for 4K content.
✕
 Remove Ads
So, instead of adding an expensive Ethernet controller, they stick with a simpler 10/100 port. It helps keep the prices down without actually affecting the experience for the average viewer. But if you’re on a faster internet plan, say 300 Mbps or more, this limitation becomes real. Your TV can’t take advantage of those extra speeds over Ethernet. It simply hits a ceiling and stops there.
Since your TV’s Wi-Fi hardware is often far more capable than its Ethernet port, a strong wireless connection can actually deliver higher speeds than a wired one. So, while it may sound counterintuitive, you’re often better off using Wi-Fi than Ethernet on a smart TV.

Why can using an excessively long ethernet patch cable — say 90 meters — inside a home cause performance problems even if it is under the 100-meter limit?
A
Ethernet cables lose their shielding effectiveness beyond 50 meters indoors
B
Signal attenuation and resistance increase with length, and wall jacks add additional length that can push past the 100-meter limit
C
Routers automatically throttle connections on cables longer than 75 meters
D
The RJ45 connector degrades the signal on cables over 80 meters
Correct! The 100-meter limit includes ALL cabling in the run — the patch cable from your device to the wall jack, the in-wall cable, and the patch cable at the other end. A 90-meter cable plus wall connections can easily exceed 100 meters total, causing signal attenuation, errors, and reduced speeds.
Not quite. The key issue is that the 100-meter ethernet limit is for the entire channel, not just the visible patch cable. In-wall runs plus patch cables at both ends all count toward that limit, so a 90-meter patch cable almost guarantees the total run will exceed 100 meters and degrade performance.

A user crimps their own RJ45 connector and notices the cable works but speeds are far below expected. What wiring mistake most commonly causes this?
A
Using T568A wiring on a T568B network
B
Untwisting the wire pairs too much before inserting them into the connector
C
Using solid-core wire instead of stranded wire in a patch cable
D
Forgetting to ground the cable shield before crimping
Correct! Untwisting too much of the wire pairs before crimping is one of the most common DIY ethernet mistakes. The twists in each pair are specifically engineered to cancel out crosstalk between pairs. Removing too much of the twist degrades signal integrity, increases crosstalk, and can drastically reduce speeds even though the cable technically works.
Not quite. The most common culprit for poor speeds in home-crimped cables is untwisting the wire pairs too far before inserting them into the RJ45 connector. Those twists are not decorative — they actively cancel electromagnetic crosstalk between pairs, and removing too many of them introduces interference that tanks performance.

What is a common misunderstanding about using a higher-category ethernet cable — such as Cat6 — with older Cat5e wall jacks and patch panels?
A
Cat6 cables physically cannot plug into Cat5e-rated keystone jacks
B
The entire link performs at the level of its lowest-rated component, so Cat6 cable with Cat5e jacks gives Cat5e performance
C
Mixing categories causes the router to disable the port entirely
D
Cat6 cables automatically downgrade their signal to match Cat5e devices
Correct! This is a classic and costly misunderstanding. A network link is only as good as its weakest component. If you run Cat6 cable but terminate it on Cat5e-rated keystone jacks or patch panels, the channel is certified only to Cat5e performance. Upgrading the cable without upgrading the termination hardware wastes money and effort.
Not quite. The correct answer highlights an important principle: the whole ethernet channel — cable, connectors, jacks, and patch panels — must be rated to the same category to achieve that category's performance. Using Cat6 cable with Cat5e jacks limits the entire link to Cat5e specs, making the cable upgrade pointless.

                        Breaking past your TV’s Ethernet speed limit
               

            Turn your TV into a gigabit-ready device
    
Amir Bohlooli / MUO
Credit: Amir Bohlooli / MUO
 So if 100 Mbps is enough for streaming, why even bother bypassing that limit in the first place? Well, there are a couple of reasons you might want to do that.
Wi-Fi can be fast, sure, but it’s still prone to interference and signal drops. If your TV is far away from the router or if there is too much interference, a wired connection is the way to go. But then, the slower speeds of the built-in Ethernet connection can be a bottleneck. For instance, if you're using your TV for cloud gaming, higher bandwidth can help maintain smoother performance. The same goes for local streaming. Playing 4K or Blu-ray remux files from a NAS or Plex server can easily exceed 100 Mbps. When that happens, the standard Ethernet port may struggle and lead to buffering.
✕
 Remove Ads
The good news is that even if your TV’s built-in Ethernet port is stuck at 100 Mbps, you’re not out of luck. A USB Ethernet adapter can help you overcome this. It works because it has its own networking chip inside, which means it doesn’t need to rely on the TV’s Ethernet controller at all. As long as your TV supports the adapter, it’ll recognize it as a standard wired connection. You’re essentially 
giving your TV a second, faster Ethernet port
.

                        Adding a faster Ethernet port to your TV is easy
               

            You don’t need much
    
If you’re already using an Ethernet connection on your smart TV, you don’t need to do anything special to upgrade. First, grab a USB-to-Ethernet adapter from Amazon. They’re quite inexpensive, usually somewhere between $10 to $25, depending on the brand and the maximum speed they support. If you go for a USB-C adapter, you'll also need a USB-A to USB-C converter, since TVs only have the standard USB-A ports.
A good USB 3.0 Ethernet adapter can support speeds of up to 1 Gbps, which is ten times faster than what most TVs offer through the built-in port. Of course, this works best if your TV has a USB 3.0 port, which most modern TVs do. But even with a USB 2.0 port, you’re still looking at a theoretical limit of 480 Mbps, which is still far beyond the 100 Mbps cap.
✕
 Remove Ads
Also, 
make sure your Ethernet cable isn’t holding you back
. If it’s a Cat 5 cable, your speed will be limited to 100 Mbps because of the cable. A basic Cat5e or Cat6 cable is more than enough for this setup. You don’t need to overthink this part unless you’re running very long cables across your home.
Once you have everything ready, plug the adapter into your TV’s USB 3.0 port. Most smart TVs do support USB Ethernet adapters out of the box, so your TV should detect the Ethernet connection automatically and connect to the internet.
Keep in mind that the USB to Ethernet adapter may not work on all TV models, so it's usually best to verify it by checking the user manual or quick web search.
Honestly, the 100 Mbps limit is not really a problem for most people. In fact, many won’t even notice it, even after using a smart TV for years. But if you’re someone who wants the fastest speed possible, this USB trick is quite easy and can work wonders.
