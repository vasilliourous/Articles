Vibe coding has changed how software developers work. I'll be the first to admit that. You can go from an idea to at least a working prototype in a fraction of the time and effort that it would otherwise take to build just about anything. However, as useful as it is, you shouldn't vibe code everything.

ChatGPT told me no when working on a project
, and that got me thinking. Just because AI can assist you in coding something doesn't mean it's a replacement for human thinking. I'd vibe code just about anything, especially a proof-of-concept or a prototype, but there are certain projects where I'm benching AI, and for good reason.

                        Security-sensitive code needs full control
               

            You can’t outsource decisions when data is at risk
    
Yadullah Abidi / MakeUseOf
Credit: Yadullah Abidi / MakeUseOf
Handing over tasks like authentication logic, encryption layer, or secrets management over to your AI is a one-way road to future trouble. Your AI model of choice may be up to the task, but your code needs better than that. When dealing with security-sensitive projects, you need to be on the lookout for security flaws, a concern AI models don't necessarily have.
Research from New York University
 and benchmark analysis from 
BaxBench
 confirm that 40 to 62 percent of AI-generated code contains security flaws. While that number has certainly improved as AI models get better at coding, if you're using a free-tier model to code security-sensitive applications without human oversight, chances are you're leaving the door open for attackers.
✕
 Remove Ads
The reason for this is also quite simple. AI models have learned how to code by training on the vast amount of public code available on the internet, including all the bad coding practices that these codebases have. So insecure patterns, outdated cryptographic functions, and misconfigured access controls are more common than you think. The AI doesn't necessarily know that MD5 would be a bad choice for password hashing simply because a lot of projects in its training data use that approach. It's not sabotaging you on purpose; it's just training on bad practices that professionals steer clear of.

                        Messing with vehicles isn’t a playground
               

            Hardware integrations where mistakes have real consequences
    
I learned not to vibe code projects that deal with vehicles or machinery in real-time the hard way, and it was ChatGPT of all things that made me realize it. If a bug in your web app crashes, you're losing out on your browser tab. If a bug in your CAN bus interface sends the wrong signal to your vehicle's throttle or brake system, the worst outcome is considerably more final.
✕
 Remove Ads
You see, embedded systems talking to vehicles run under timing constraints, safety measures, and hardware overrides that an AI has no real feel for. The code needs to be absolutely deterministic and has to handle edge cases and hardware failures in a very specific manner, often dictated by automotive standards. Using AI-generated code in such instances means you're relying on its understanding of vehicle safety behaviors and understanding of how OEM-made features work. Considering these systems are often protected by the OEMs who manufacture them, there's a very low chance your AI model has a good understanding of what it's doing.
In any case, a mistake here isn't something you fix in your next vibe coding session. It's a safety incident that could get you to a hospital. Sure, 
Gemini 3's vibe coding tools can help you make your dream apps
, but just because it can code them doesn't mean they're right for every situation.

                        Medical software has zero margin for error
               

            When bugs can directly impact human lives
    
Credit: Brandon Miniman / MakeUseOf
Medical software might look quite straightforward on the surface, but once you dive into the regulatory requirements, it's a nightmare. Code that runs on or works with a medical device has to meet frameworks like IEC 62304, which requires traceability, verification, and documentation at every step of development — something vibe coding skips by definition.
The practical risks are quite serious here. Even the smallest bugs can cause unpredictable behavior at an unexpected moment, which has the potential to cause serious medical harm depending on what device you're talking about. The same concepts that apply to hardware interfacing with vehicles apply here. Bugs or flaws in code are unacceptable, security is paramount, and human oversight isn't optional.
✕

                        Money systems demand absolute precision
               

            Financial code needs strict validation and traceability
    
Image taken by Yadullah Abidi | No attribution required.
Coding for the financial and banking sectors is a whole different beast. There are sector-specific concepts and conditions that your code needs to follow, not to mention the highest levels of security. Solving problems here requires paranoid, deliberate thinking at every layer of the system, not the casual prototyping approach that vibe coding brings with it.
For example, there's a concept called idempotency — the idea that if the same payment operation is triggered twice, only one payment should go through. It sounds simple in theory, but once you start factoring in race conditions, retry logic, and ledger consistency, it quickly develops into a complicated situation where you have to account for everything to produce one predictable outcome every single time.

The smallest mistakes can be just as expensive as the biggest ones here. Authorization flaws can drain millions of dollars in minutes, double-crediting bugs can come up under production load, and AI-generated code's overly permissive tendencies can burn billions before the fix can be shipped. Not to mention the fact that payment systems and trading infrastructure also carry regulatory audit requirements. A random piece of AI logic in the middle of an otherwise massive codebase isn't just risky — it can be quite expensive.
✕

            Some projects can’t afford guesswork
    
None of this means vibe coding is bad. It means it's a tool, and like every tool, it requires an expert to handle certain situations. AI isn't a replacement for human thinking, so if the thinking isn't in the right place, you might end up with a buggy, inefficient, and potentially dangerous piece of code on your hands.
