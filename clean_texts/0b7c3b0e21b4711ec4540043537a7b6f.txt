                        CachyOS April 2026's update makes sudo even more secure
               

            Plus, a change from Octopi to Shelly
    
Over on the 
CachyOS website
, the team details everything we can expect from the April 2026 update. There's a smorgasbord of changes with this month's update, including replacing Octopi with Shelly. Shelly adds a GUI for installing packages from sources such as the AUR, Flathub, and even AppImages, which should make setting up a PC a lot easier for people unfamiliar with Arch Linux. Also, CachyOS will now enable os-prober by default so that distros on other partitions can find it easier.
However, one of the cooler features has to do with superuser commands:
chwd
 automatically configures 
fingerprint readers
 for 
sudo
 prompts, detects specific Intel CPUs for better power saving, and correctly handle laptop edge cases.
✕
 Remove Ads
Yes, you read that right. Instead of typing in your password to confirm a sudo command, you can instead use a fingerprint scanner to give the terminal the proper credentials to verify you. Given how much harder it is to replicate your fingerprint than your passwords, it sounds like an excellent addition for the security-minded user.

Let's clear a few myths up here as I am getting fed up with this old news reporting from XDA and just jumping on a hype train that left the station years ago and selling old features as new ones.
Fingerprint authentication was always possible in CachyOS. They just disabled the stack from the kernel underneath it, remember all Linux distros under the hood are essentially the same thing, a better way to imagine them are like Windows with certain services disabled or enabled, say Windows Lite, Windows Gaming, Windows Server, etc, etc. All that it would have taken to be enabled is literally maybe 4 lines of CLi. A quick google or an AI agent could very quickly have told those who do not know how the process of how to re-enable it.
My 12 year old ThinkPad which I use for PenTesting (I work in CyberSec) has had this feature its entire lifespan, this may have been before this OP was even using a PC.
They simply enabled a feature that has existed for well over a decade. Linux has had:
fprintd
pam_fprintd.so
full PAM integration
GUI enrollment tools
Now for the people in the comments claiming fingerprint security is dangerous. This is more nuanced than they realise. The first login after the terminal has been dormant for a long time HAS to be the password or PIN(on some distros), therefore if you are arrested and forced to use your fingerprint then this is on you for not creating a short time-out whereby the password/PIN is cached. Fingerprint is NOT supposed to protect the device first that still belongs to the password.
Then there is the fact that if they have your hard-drive already with no/disabled TPM2.0 and no drive encryption enabled then the password is utterly useless and not needed. Most distro's are only just tying drive encryption and TPM together as standard, again you have been able to enable it for a long time but it had to be done manually.
The safety of your device is YOUR responsibility. Linux just doesn't hold your hand and it isn't designed to do so.
