When people get into self-hosting, the conversation almost naturally starts at the hardware level. Old office PCs, 
mini PCs
, NAS boxes, rack servers, power-efficient 
CPUs
, and drive capacity are all tangible parts of the hobby, so it makes total sense. They are easy to compare, easy to look at, and a logical starting point.

But hardware is only part of what makes a self-hosted setup pleasant to live with. Once you actually get a service online, a more practical issue starts to matter: how you actually reach everything, organize it, and keep it manageable as your setup grows. That is where one of the most overlooked parts of self-hosting comes in. Getting a domain is one of the most useful upgrades you can make, even if it is much less exciting than buying new hardware.

                        Getting a domain is one of the best QoL changes you can make
               

            No more memorizing port numbers
    
 A domain does not make your server faster or add more storage, but it makes your setup feel far more coherent. A lot of self-hosted environments start out as a collection of IP addresses and port numbers, and at first, that's totally fine, but once you add more services and potentially nodes, things can start to feel a lot more cluttered and clouded than they otherwise should.
A domain gives you a stable naming layer on top of all that. Instead of remembering exactly where each service lives via a local IP and port, you can give everything a clean, readable address. Add subdomain access on top of that, and things become stupidly simple to access and link to, even through VM migrations and internal IP changes.

                        If you plan to share your services to non-techie people, it's practically a must
               

            Plus bonuses to secure access
    
Having a real, registered domain makes your services so much easier to share. A clean address is much easier to trust, remember, and explain than an internal IP and a random port number, especially when you're talking to friends or family members who don't understand what an IP address is. That alone could be intimidating enough to scare people off of using your stuff.
A real domain also unlocks a more polished version of self-hosting. Your reverse proxies, HTTPS certificates, secure remote access, and app integrations all get easier to manage when you are working with proper set of hostnames instead of raw IP addresses. It gives your setup an actual front door, even if the way traffic reaches your server changes later.

                        Local domains are a free way to do a similar thing
               

            If you don't plan on exposing things to the internet

 The strongest counter-point is that you do not actually need to buy a domain to get cleaner service names. For a local-only setup, internal DNS can already solve a lot of the mess. With the right router, Pi-hole, AdGuard Home, or firewall appliance, you can just as easily map friendly local addresses to your services if a full-on registered domain seems like overkill. If your self-hosted environment is only meant to work on your home network, and you have no interest in exposing anything externally, then a purchased domain isn't necessary.

                        Domains are so cheap, it's hard to argue against it
               

            They can be a few bucks a year
    
 The problem is that local naming only solves part of the issue. It helps with organization, but it does not give you the same portability or long-term flexibility if you eventually do want remote access. They're also so incredibly inexpensive, coming in at a few bucks a year if you choose something a bit esoteric. Obviously, anything even remotely recognizable will cost you a bit more, and it's up to you to decide whether that's worth it to you or not. I'd say if the choice is between a slightly pricey domain and a new, snall hardware upgrade, the latter choice is going to pay much bigger dividends in terms of ROI.
