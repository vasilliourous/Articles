Have you ever stopped to think about the enormity of the internet, the sheer amount of data packets that are transmitted worldwide every minute, let alone every day? It's a staggering amount, and that it functions at all, while still getting the data we need where it needs to go, is a technical marvel. 
Internet routing
 is the unsung hero of our 
digital infrastructure
, designed to route around issues and find the best path at scale.
✕

Or at least, when it's working correctly. The paths are largely controlled autonomously between routers, which means they can be subverted or rerouted to other networks to intercept communications, or accidentally if the wrong settings are used on large-scale networks. These routes have 
no native way to secure them
, and decades of engineering effort have been devoted to adding measures to limit how they can be attacked. 
And 
Switzerland claims to have cracked the code for securing these routes
. Not by adding to the existing system, but by completely replacing one part of it, the Border Gateway Protocol, or BGP. But first, we need to take a little trip down memory lane.

                        What is BGP, and why was it designed in the manner it was?
               

            Border Gateway Protocol is one of the foundations of our digital world
    
The routers that transfer our data across the internet use three main protocols to decide how the individual data packets are transferred. BGP is the relevant one here because that's the insecure, designed-for-routing-at-scale protocol that the Swiss have found a replacement for.
BGP (Border Gateway Protocol):
 Used for routing between large networks
                        
OSPF (Open Shortest Path First)
: Used within large networks to find the best path based on link states
                        
RIP (Routing Information Protocol)
: An older protocol that uses distance vector algorithms
                        
✕
 Remove Ads
Let's use a simple analogy for this. If the data packet was a pizza, that would mean the routers could choose any route to your house, regardless of how long it takes, and of course, it has to reroute around traffic or closed streets. The thing with BGP is that there is no cryptographic chain of custody for any data packet. In the context of your pizza, it could go to a different driver, or be given to someone else first, before it gets to your house.
You wouldn't know, and it's all built on trust. BGP can be hijacked, like in 2008 when 
Pakistan Telecom used BGP to route YouTube's address block into a black hole
. That's like 
Pi-hole removing YouTube from your home network
, but on a global scale, because the BGP black hole was accidentally propagated worldwide.

            Every tool trying to secure it has to fight 40 years of technical debt
    
The protocols used for at-scale routing have had patches and extensions to mitigate these issues, but as evidenced, they don't always work. It's impressive enough that a 40-year-old protocol holds up under the immense weight of data the modern world transmits, but trying to stay secure on top of that is a fool's errand. Something else needs to be done, and that's what researchers at ETH Zurich did.

                        What the Swiss did instead was start over
               

            Scalability, Control, and Isolation On Next-Generation Networks, aka SCION, was born
    
Source: Wikicommons
✕
 Remove Ads
Scalability, Control, and Isolation On Next-Generation Networks, or SCION, came about at ETH Zurich around 2009, after Adrian Perrig gained tenure and the freedom to work on things he cared about. While I could spend the rest of today talking about why tenure is good, and research for research's sake is also good, that's not the focus here. SCION is, and it's a rethinking of the protocols that underpin the internet.
Even before attending university for his bachelor's degree, Perrig was obsessed with internet security. The issues with BGP and other protocols have been known since the 1980s, and they still exist now. Everything that followed was built on an insecure foundation, and SCION was a redesign from the architectural level instead of trying to fix something that was fundamentally broken.

            What SCION does differently
    
SCION combines three approaches to packet routing, making it secure and fast. The first is to create multiple parallel paths at once, so if something fails along the chain, the system can switch more quickly than a human can react. The next step is to enable each country, region, or even organization to create its own local trust roots, so it doesn't rely on a small list of trusted authorities, and a compromise in one area can't propagate to another. And every router has a cryptographic signature, so packets can't be rerouted to an undefined router.
✕
 Remove Ads
So we have:

                                        Multi-path routing
                        

                                        Isolation domains
                        

                                        Cryptographic path validation
                        
Instead of adding these as fixes, they're built into the protocol from the core. It's secure, strong, and validated as fast. It's used in Switzerland's interbank clearing system, with 200 billion francs routed each day.

            Why hasn't it caught on outside of Switzerland?
    
The conditions inside Swiss governance make it uniquely suited to change entire protocols like this. The internet at large runs on standards, and SCION isn't ratified by the standards agencies that the rest of the world uses. Yet. The process hasn't even begun, at least not for the agencies that matter to organizations. And only one company currently packages SCION for use by ISPs and other users. That's a problem, because it needs to be larger to reach the necessary depth the internet requires.

Nobody wants to be first, even if the Swiss banking industry has proven this works at a significant scale. The internet has already found ways to account for BGP weirdness and delay, and any infrastructure project is a huge undertaking. But SCION is being adopted into networking libraries, so the future might be very different.

            Nobody thinks about foundations until they crack, and one day that will come for the internet
    
We talk a lot about encryption and other ways to secure individual data as it travels over the internet, but it's all built on shaky foundations. The core of the internet, as it is currently implemented, is insecure and practically unsecurable. Every security patch is like putting a band-aid on a leaking boat, showing the years of technical debt that needs to be addressed if we are to have secure communications. Or maybe we don't need that, with the rise of software-defined networking and better encryption methods. Either way, at some point, decisions will have to be made.

You are not entirely correct about bgp and security. Idea of crypto signed bgp announcements is not new, RFC 7454 is more than 10 years old and it was not even first. It takes time for networks (especially for some less technical ISPs) to implement it, but when everyone would implement it - it would be impossible to hijack routes. And right now for some networks you already must support route authentication to peer with them. It just takes time.
Traffic engineering is also something that is not new, every big company do that and rebalance traffic based on whatever they think important now (another feature that scion claims they do support and modern networks doesn't).
And for the first problem - there is no threat model for some one routing arbitrary traffic through them for the sole reason that in modern networks it is encrypted and even if you can dump it - it is useless for you. And if you are worried about someone doing something weird - macsec is also a thing for last 10+ years. 
So world ignores scion because it doesn't try to solve a problem that a lot of people have. It solved problem or certain companies who want something that would calm down their paranoia and who don't want to check if their fears have any actual base. For world to have a look at it and start adapting it over current network it must solve real problems in a better way and not to have any drawbacks, and for scion that is not true - it have about double the overhead of classical ip networks, there is no hardware support for it, it also seems that routing decisions takes much more processing power and so on.
