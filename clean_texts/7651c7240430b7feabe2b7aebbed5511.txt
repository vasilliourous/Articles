Modern smart homes often have around 30, 50, or even 100+ Wi-Fi devices when you count all the bulbs, plugs, and cameras. Most consumer routers start to choke or drop connections once you hit that 30-device threshold because they're just not made to handle so many devices trying to communicate at once. This means that, as a result, your expensive Wi-Fi 7 phone is getting slow speeds because it's sharing a radio channel with 40 cheap smart plugs that phone home every five seconds.

While most people see an old router as e-waste, it's actually the secret to a stable, secure, and high-performance smart home. A smart home shouldn't fight for bandwidth with your four clay in Netflix stream by offloading your smart devices to a dedicated hardware lane. You fix the congestion and security issues that plague modern Wi-Fi. The solution to the problem here is using your old router as a dedicated IoT subnetwork, and you'll finally stop encountering the "too many devices" crash.

                        The benefits of using a second router
               

            It can fix the dreaded lag you always encounter
    
One of the main benefits of using your old router for your smart home devices is security. If you've picked up a load of cheap IoT smart home devices, it's likely they have questionable security and phone home to overseas servers quite often. Creating network segmentation with your old router creates a sort of security shield. By plugging your old router's WAN port into your main router's LAN port, you create an inner and outer network.
The result is that even if a smart bulb is hacked, the attacker is trapped on the old router's network and then, as a result, they can't see your PC, your NAS, or your banking data on the main router. By using an old router to segment your network, you're actually creating a safer home network and ensuring that no matter what happens, all your information stays private.
✕
 Remove Ads
Another great benefit is clearing the 2.4 GHz traffic jam that might be occurring. Most IoT devices only support 2.4 GHz, which is notoriously crowded and prone to interference from other devices, such as microwaves or even devices in your neighbor's home.
The old router is strictly on the 2.4GHz band for your smart home, and then move all of your high-speed devices like phones, laptops, and consoles to the 5GHz or 6GHz bands on your primary router. As a result, your main Wi-Fi becomes a clean room for high-performance tasks and devices that absolutely need faster internet speeds, while the old router handles the constant chatter of the smart home on a congested band.

You can even create a permanent access point using an old router. Putting your router into access point mode 
lets you place the 
second router in a dead zone

                        How to set up your second router
               

            It's easier than you think
    
✕
 Remove Ads
 To get started, it's worth keeping in mind that this is a five-minute setup process, but despite that, you aren't just plugging things in. You're actually configuring a network bridge that needs to be invisible to your main traffic while remaining rock-solid for your IoT devices.
Setting up your second router should take less time than making a pot of coffee, but make sure you avoid the dreaded IP address that can knock your entire house offline.
Start with a hardware reset. Don't assume 
your old settings
 will work while the router is powered on. Hold the pinhole reset button for 30 seconds. Continue holding it while plugging in the power for another 30 seconds, and finally keep holding it while plugging the power back in for a final 30 seconds. This ensures a deep factory wipe.
Next, change the IP. Log in to the old router using a laptop and an Ethernet cable. You need to change it to a LAN IP to sit inside your main network's range, but outside the DHCP pool. Make sure that you disable DHCP too, which is the most important step. In a single network, there can only be one brain, which is the DHCP server handing out IP addresses. Look for the DHCP server in the LAN settings and flip it to off or disabled. If you leave this on, the old router will try to assign your phone an IP address that the main router doesn't recognize, resulting in a "connected, no internet" error continuously.

Next is how you plug in the router, which determines how it behaves. You can either go as a bridge, meaning you connect it LAN to LAN. This means you connect an Ethernet cable from a LAN port on your main router to a LAN port on the old router. This makes the old router a switch/AP. It's the fastest, most stable way to expand your Wi-Fi.
✕
 Remove Ads
If you want to use it as a shield, then go LAN to WAN. Connect a LAN port on your main router to a WAN port on the old router. This creates a firewall between your networks, and devices on the old router can see the internet, but they cannot see or hack the devices on your main router. And with that, you're set up and ready to go.

                        Don't let your old device become e-waste
               

            You have the solution to laggy internet already at home
    
You don't need a $500 mesh system to
 fix your smart home lag
. You just need to stop making your smart devices fight with your actual fast devices, where you need that faster internet connection. If you have countless devices all connected to the same internet network fighting for airtime just so they can phone home, then of course your phone isn't going to be able to download an app as quickly as it should be able to. Instead of throwing out your old router and deeming it e-waste, use it as a high-performance lane for your home's growing digital brain.

It's not about adding in the old one because the new one isn't good enough. It's about splitting the available 2.4ghz spectrum and radios. I try not to have wifi IOT devices, but there are still a few things that don't have good alternatives. One good AP won't make your IOT devices not suck when they're all the same channel. If you don't need good range and capacity on 2.4ghz, one AP running the full channel width is good.
