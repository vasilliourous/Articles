                        A smaller home lab needs fewer moving parts
               

            Simple backup jobs are easier to trust over time
    
The biggest problem with Proxmox Backup Server in a small home lab isn't that it's bad. It's that it's another system to build, maintain, update, monitor, and understand before it can protect anything. That sounds obvious, but it's easy to ignore when the tool is purpose-built for the job. A backup platform still becomes another service that can break at exactly the wrong moment.
✕
 Remove Ads
In my case, the safer path was also the less glamorous one. I already had storage available, a NAS, and an understanding of where my important data lived. Instead of adding a dedicated backup server, I focused on making the built-in Proxmox backup jobs boring and predictable. Boring is underrated when the goal is being able to recover from a mistake without turning the evening into a forensic exercise.
That changed the way I judged my backup setup. I stopped asking whether it looked impressive on paper and started asking whether I could explain it while tired. The answer matters because backups are usually tested at inconvenient times. A system that's simple enough to understand under pressure is often more reliable than one with more features than the situation requires.

                        Built-in Proxmox backups already cover the important basics
               

            The best backup plan is the one you verify
    
For a small home lab, the built-in Proxmox backup system covers a surprising amount of ground. It can back up VMs and containers on a schedule, send them to a storage target, and keep enough restore points to recover from normal mistakes. That covers accidental deletions, bad updates, failed experiments, and most of the little disasters that actually happen at home. It doesn't feel fancy, but it handles the core job.
The real improvement came when I stopped obsessing over the backup engine and started caring more about the restore path. I wanted backups that were visible, easy to rotate, and easy to test. A backup that exists in a beautiful system but never gets restored is still mostly a decorative object. The restore process is where a backup setup either earns its place or gets exposed as shelfware.
✕
 Remove Ads
Using simpler jobs also improved my backup habits. I could check whether the latest backup had finished, confirm that the storage target was reachable, and run occasional restore tests without navigating to another dedicated appliance. That made the whole thing feel less like infrastructure upkeep and more like routine maintenance. In a small setup, that difference matters because the entire operations department is one person with coffee and a questionable chair.

                        Proxmox Backup Server still has a strong case
               

            Deduplication and retention are genuinely useful features
    
There are good reasons people recommend Proxmox Backup Server. Deduplication can save significant storage when you have many similar VMs, frequent backup jobs, or several Proxmox nodes feeding into the same target. The retention controls are also much more elegant than a pile of basic backup files. Add encryption, pruning, garbage collection, and verification, and it becomes clear why the tool exists.
It also gives you a cleaner long-term backup story if your home lab has grown into something closer to a production environment. If you're hosting services for others, running multiple nodes, or maintaining a large fleet of VMs, centralized backup management stops being a decoration. At that point, the extra system has a real job. It reduces chaos instead of adding to it.
✕
 Remove Ads
That's why I don't think Proxmox Backup Server is the wrong tool. I think it's the wrong default for some of us. Home lab advice often drifts toward the most capable answer instead of the most appropriate one. The capable answer looks better in screenshots, but the appropriate answer is the one you'll keep using six months later.
Proxmox Backup Server makes more sense once your home lab grows beyond a few important VMs and containers. Multiple Proxmox nodes, frequent backup jobs, strict retention needs, and a separate machine that can stay online as a dedicated backup target all make its extra structure easier to justify. For a smaller setup, though, the simpler built-in backup workflow can be easier to verify and restore from. The right backup system is the one you’ll actually maintain, not just the one with the strongest feature list.

                        Reliability improves when the backup plan fits the lab
               

            Extra features can make recovery harder than expected
    
The reason I still skip Proxmox Backup Server is that its benefits didn't outweigh its overhead in my setup. I didn't have enough VMs for deduplication to feel transformative. I didn't need a 
dedicated backup interface
 to manage a handful of workloads. I needed something I could configure once, inspect quickly, and restore from without having to remember the fine print of another service.
That choice made my backup plan more reliable by reducing the number of assumptions involved. I wasn't depending on another server being healthy, another datastore being mounted correctly, or another maintenance routine being remembered. I still had to think about storage, retention, and testing, but those concerns were easier to see. Fewer layers meant fewer places for problems to hide.
✕
 Remove Ads
Skipping Proxmox Backup Server also forced me to be more honest about what I was protecting. Some VMs deserved regular full backups because rebuilding them would be annoying. Others were easier to recreate than preserve. Once I made that distinction, the backup plan stopped being a blanket and became a map. That map was simpler, and it made recovery decisions faster.

            A backup setup should match the real risk
    
Small home labs don't need to copy enterprise habits just because the tools are available. Proxmox Backup Server is powerful, polished, and absolutely worth considering when the environment is large enough to benefit from it. For my setup, though, it added more structure than I needed and more maintenance than I wanted. The simpler path made my backups easier to understand, test, and trust.
That's the part that matters most. A reliable backup setup isn't the one with the longest feature list; it's the one you can actually operate when something breaks. In a small home lab, that often means trimming the stack rather than adding another carefully named server. Proxmox Backup Server is still a great tool, but for me, skipping it made the whole safety net stronger.

I use PBS on the same Proxmox host and with local storage. I don't think the author actually understands the real advantage of PBS.

The maintenance needs are also minimal in my experience (and practically none if you enable automatic updates). Where PBS really shines though is incremental backups, thanks to its chunk-based storage. Wholly disagree with what the author wrote here. I concur with other people in the comments where he _clearly_ has missed the entire point of PBS. You can have your host manage your backups 
provided
 that your host doesn't have or 
can't, ever
 run into issues.
If you architect your backup solution such that your host can die, you spawn a new Proxmox host with 
literally
 nearly anything else, and then you can restore your LXCs and VMs from PBS, mostly 
regardless
 of what host you're running. (It gets a bit more complicated if you have hardware passthrough as your new host will need to mirror the physical hardware passthrough for the restore to be seamless, else; you'd have to update/fix that.)
I used to have my Proxmox host manage its own backups.
Now I ship the backups off to PBS, running on my QNAP NAS (because it's running a Debian container), and then that primary PBS is then backed up (sync'd) to a secondary PBS server, in case the primary PBS fails and/or dies.
This has also made it so that if I want to migrate LXCs/VMs between nodes that 
aren't
 in a Proxmox cluster, something that's possible (although you 
could
 do it with host-managed backups as well, but it's less "automated".)
And if/when you have to spawn a new Proxmox host, rather than having to configure an manage 
it's
 own backup, by having PBS, you can point your new node to your existing PBS, and away you go.
Lastly, PBS has chunk-level data deduplication, whereas host-managed backups, do not.
I think that my deduplication factor is something like 13.something right now.
If you were using host-managed backups, every will take the full amount of disk space.

So...PBS has its own issues...but I respectfully disagree with some of the premises here...
First, if a VM isn't worth backing up, delete it now. If you can't delete it now, it's because it matters and it's worth backing up. There are certain exceptions (a VPN server or a *clearly* dev experiment for example), but on the whole, if it's worth keeping, it's worth protecting.
Second, PBS's weakness is that it's basically-unable to use network storage. Yes, you *can* vi /etc/fstab and add a CIFS/NFS share, but you're screwed if you lose the VM; I speak from firsthand experience in trying to restore from a rebuilt VM with a re-pointed NAS share...it doesn't work (not that I think PVE's integrated tools would fare much better).
Finally, PBS doesn't back up to S3 - not reliably, anyway. It's in a beta state, but it's a mess of its own. Maybe by v5 PBS will treat a cloud repo as a first class citizen, but not right now.
PBS *is* great for its intended purpose - incremental backups to locally attached storage; it's MUCH faster than Veeam, especially for individual file restores...but in environments that matter to me, I pair it with free Veeam. Depending on the nature of the failure, the combination of the two will usually ensure that at least one backup can be used to restore.

Surprise, surprise... A software targeting enterprises don't fit in your small homelab.
The homelabbers who do use PBS are often also those who have a fairly sized lab, and those who run their homelab like an enterprise. After all, the main premise of a homelab is to "do it yourself" without having companies control your data (or spy on/sell it; you are your own enterprise provider, providing compute, storage, network, etc. You're your own system administrator/network engineer/cloud architecture engineer/backup engineer/analyst/QA/CEO/end user.
