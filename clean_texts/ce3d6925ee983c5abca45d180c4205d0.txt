Linux has always been powerful; it's finally starting to look the part too.

                        Bazaar
               

            One place to find, install, and manage all your Flatpaks
    
The usual way of installing 
Flatpaks
 can be a bit clunky. You open the browser, visit 
Flathub
, search for the app you want to download, grab its installation command, and finally run that in your terminal. Wouldn't it be better to just have a proper graphical storefront where you can find and install apps in one place? Well, that's exactly what you get with 
Bazaar
.
The app offers a clean and modern storefront for discovering and downloading Flatpaks. It's really fast, and you can queue up as many apps to install as you like without the app choking up. It also doubles as a management frontend, allowing you to update and delete all your Flatpaks—even the ones you didn't explicitly download through Bazaar. Another nice touch is that each app page includes all the relevant links you need to research the app and its developer, including a donation link (if provided) in case you want to support the developer for their contributions.
✕
 Remove Ads
Now, yes, technically, you can add a plugin or flip a toggle in most software stores—like GNOME Software or KDE Discover—and that would let you install Flatpaks graphically. However, in my experience, Bazaar feels a lot smoother and snappier than any other Linux storefront. In fact, I'd go as far as saying that Bazaar should become the default option for graphically installing all apps on Linux—both Flatpaks and regular packages from the official repos.
To install Bazaar, you first need to 
install and set up Flatpak
 on your system and then run this command:
flatpak install flathub io.github.kolunmi.Bazaar

                        Buzz
               

            Transcribe local audio files and YouTube links—no cloud needed
    
✕
 Remove Ads
Buzz
 is a local speech-to-text transcription tool. You get a simple graphical interface where you pick a Whisper-based AI model, download it, and then let it process your audio entirely on your own hardware—no cloud, no subscription, no audio leaving your machine. You can load a local audio file, paste a URL, or even drop in a YouTube link and it'll pull the audio and transcribe it directly.
Buzz can also 
translate
 your audio before the transcription process, but I personally haven't had much luck with this feature. I believe it failed for me because I was missing the necessary language packs on my PC. For straight English transcription though, it's been flawless.
Personally, I have Buzz monitoring a folder on my computer. Whenever I record audio on my phone, I use 
Syncthing
 to automatically send that recording to that specific PC folder. When a new file shows up, Buzz automatically transcribes it and creates a text file for me.
There's also a live transcription mode where Buzz transcribes speech in real time as you talk. It works, but it's been a bit iffy in my testing. It would cut me off mid-sentence to transcribe what I said and then start listening again, causing chunks of audio to get lost. So I personally prefer using it for transcribing full audio files.
✕
 Remove Ads
How fast it transcribes depends entirely on your hardware. With my RTX 3060, Buzz can transcribe 20 minutes of audio in under 20 seconds. The model I use is Faster Whisper Large v3—it strikes a great balance between speed and accuracy. You can go with a smaller, lighter model if you want faster results, but you'll take a hit on accuracy. It also supports Whisper, 
Whisper.cpp
, and any compatible model hosted on Hugging Face.
Without a GPU, transcription will still work using the CPU, but it'll be painfully slow. In those situations, you can 
plug in an OpenAI API key
 and offload the processing to OpenAI's servers. It's faster, but it does mean your audio is leaving your machine—something worth keeping in mind if privacy matters to you.
Buzz is available as both a 
Flatpak
 and a 
Snap
, so if you've already got Bazaar set up, you can install it from there.

                        Yazi
               

            A terminal file manager that's actually a pleasure to use
    
If you're tired of 
ls-ing and cd-ing
 your way through the Linux terminal, 
Yazi
 is exactly what you need. It's one of the 
best terminal-based file managers
 I've personally used. It looks clean and modern—but that's not just cosmetic. Yazi is thoughtfully designed to be as functional as it is good-looking.
By default, it opens in a 
Miller columns
 layout, which shows you where you came from, where you are, and what's inside the selected directory all at once. It makes navigating a deep filesystem feel natural rather than disorienting. For navigation, you have 
Vim-style
 shortcuts—use H-J-K-L to move around—as well as mouse-support. There's even a built-in cheat sheet behind F1, so you're not expected to memorize everything upfront.

Where Yazi really stands out is its feature set. You get in-pane image previews that actually render inside the terminal, file previews without having to open anything, and tabs so you can keep multiple directories open at the same time. Bulk renaming is built in, and there's a Lua plugin system if you want to extend its functionality further. It also integrates natively with 
ripgrep
, 
fzf
, and 
zoxide
