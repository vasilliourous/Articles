I stopped using Gmail around 8 years ago. The decision did not arrive as a reaction to a single incident or policy change but as a gradual shift in how I thought about email as a system. Gmail worked well then and continues to work well now. What changed for me was not the quality of the product but the role it played in a larger ecosystem that collects and correlates data across nearly every digital interaction (which sounds abstract until you notice how often you click "Sign in with Google").
✕

Email sits in a unique position because it quietly becomes the backbone of your online identity, linking accounts, storing transaction records, and acting as a fallback mechanism whenever something goes wrong elsewhere. Once that becomes obvious, it becomes difficult to ignore that Gmail operates inside an environment designed for 
data harvesting
 and I wanted my email to remain a contained tool rather than an entry point into everything.

                        Looking for a simpler model
               

            Choosing a practical alternative approach
    
The search for an alternative was grounded in practicality rather than ideology, since I still needed something that behaved like a normal email service without reinventing everything from scratch (no desire to self-host a mail server and spend weekends debugging
 SPF records
). That search led me to Proton AG and their offering, Proton Mail, which presented itself as a familiar interface built on a different set of assumptions about data handling.
Proton Mail does not attempt to redesign email, which makes the transition easier at a surface level, but its underlying model changes how messages are stored and accessed.
Messages between Proton users are encrypted by default, while communication with external recipients can be encrypted when necessary, and the entire service operates under Swiss privacy laws that impose additional constraints on data access. These details do not alter the act of writing or receiving email, yet they significantly alter the conditions under which that data exists.

                        The migration was slow by design
               

            Gradual transition without breaking access
    
Moving away from Gmail required more patience than I initially expected because email addresses are embedded across a wide range of services that accumulate over time.
Instead of attempting a complete switch, I ran Proton Mail alongside Gmail and gradually migrated accounts that mattered, starting with financial services, infrastructure providers, and platforms tied to ongoing work. This incremental process reduced the risk of breaking access to something important and allowed the transition to happen without disruption.

It also made it clear that email migration is not about moving messages from one inbox to another but about updating the web of connections that depend on that address. The process stretched over months, which turned out to be necessary rather than excessive.

                        Living outside the Google ecosystem
               

            Reducing dependency on Google services

 Leaving Gmail introduces a broader question about dependence on Google services, since email often sits alongside tools like Google Drive and Google Calendar in a tightly integrated workflow. Replacing everything at once would have added unnecessary complexity, so I treated each service independently and avoided forcing a complete migration before suitable alternatives were available.
Over time, Proton expanded its ecosystem with tools like 
Proton Drive
, Proton Calendar, and the new Proton Docs, which made it easier to consolidate certain parts of my workflow without recreating the same level of interdependence found in the Google stack (or at least not the same kind).

                        I still keep a Gmail account (unfortunately)
               

            Maintaining access to legacy systems
    
Even though Proton Mail became my primary inbox, I never removed my Gmail account entirely because it remains tied to earlier phases of my digital life, particularly university systems and accounts that were created long before I considered switching providers. These connections are not always worth untangling, especially when they function without issue and do not require active use.

The role of Gmail shifted as a result, since it now acts as a secondary inbox for legacy access rather than a central hub for communication. Important conversations, new registrations, and ongoing work all happen through Proton Mail, while Gmail exists as a compatibility layer that allows me to interact with systems that still expect a Google account. This separation has proven to be practical, since it avoids unnecessary migration effort while still keeping my primary workflow within a more controlled environment.

                        Proton Mail gets it right
               

            Focused design without unnecessary layers
    
The most noticeable change after switching is the absence of additional layers built around the inbox, since Proton Mail focuses on core functionality without attempting to extend into adjacent behaviors. The interface remains straightforward, and the experience revolves around reading and writing email without introducing elements designed to capture attention or guide user behavior.
Encryption is central to this approach, with messages secured both at rest and in transit, and communication between Proton users handled automatically without additional configuration (no toggles to remember). 
External communication 
can also be encrypted when required, which provides flexibility without complicating the default workflow. This design keeps the system consistent, even if it introduces certain limitations compared to more feature-rich alternatives.

                        The trade-offs you notice
               

            Where limitations become more visible
    
The differences become more apparent in areas where encryption imposes constraints, particularly in search functionality, since the server cannot process message contents in the same way as a traditional email provider. Proton has addressed this with client-side indexing, which improves usability, but the experience still differs from the near-instant search capabilities offered by Gmail.

Integration also requires adjustment, since Proton Mail does not connect as broadly with third-party services, and using desktop clients such as Mozilla Thunderbird involves an additional bridge application that manages encryption locally. This setup works reliably but introduces an extra component that needs to be maintained.

                        Paying for the product changes the relationship
               

            Subscriptions align incentives with users
    
The business model behind Proton Mail influences how the service behaves, since it relies on subscriptions rather than advertising, which aligns its incentives more closely with the user. This creates a clearer boundary between functionality and data collection, since the service does not depend on extracting behavioral insights to generate revenue.

With Gmail, the relationship is shaped by its role within a larger ecosystem that benefits from aggregated data, whereas Proton Mail positions itself as a standalone tool designed to perform a specific function. This distinction becomes more meaningful over time, especially when considering how much personal information passes through an email account.

                        How it holds up after 8 years
               

            Long-term reliability and steady improvements
    
Proton Mail has evolved significantly since I first started using it, with improvements in interface design, mobile applications, and overall performance that bring it closer to the level of refinement expected from a modern email service. The addition of related tools like Proton Drive and Proton Calendar has made it easier to maintain a consistent workflow without relying on external providers for basic functionality.

Despite these improvements, the service retains its original focus, which is evident in its limited scope compared to more expansive platforms. This focus contributes to a sense of stability, since the service evolves without attempting to redefine its purpose.

            Why I never went back
    
The decision to leave Gmail was not driven by dissatisfaction with its capabilities but by a preference for a system that aligns more closely with how I think about data ownership and privacy.
Proton Mail introduces a small amount of additional effort in exchange for greater control, and over time, that trade-off becomes part of the routine rather than an obstacle. After 8 years, Proton Mail feels like a natural default, while Gmail feels like a system built around a different set of priorities.
Maintaining a secondary Gmail account for legacy access has proven to be a practical compromise, allowing me to retain compatibility where necessary without returning to a workflow that depends on it as a primary tool.

Oh, the irony is truly delicious. I just had to click "Sign in with Google" to leave this comment, because after a decade of playing "privacy crusader" with Proton Mail, I’ve finally realized that being a voluntary digital hermit is just exhausting.
I spent years acting like I was some high-stakes international spy hiding state secrets, when in reality, my most sensitive data was just a grocery list and a lukewarm email from my electric company. At 37, I’ve finally seen the light: it turns out convenience actually is king. I’m back in the warm, all-knowing embrace of the Google ecosystem—Gmail, Docs, Gemini—where everything just works.
And spare me the performative outrage about Google "tracking" your flight details. If you’re genuinely having a panic attack because Google had the audacity to suggest your boarding pass, that’s not a corporate surveillance issue—that’s a you issue. Unless you’re an investigative journalist uncovering a global conspiracy or a high-ranking politician, nobody cares about your search history. You aren't that interesting, and neither am I.
We’re all running away from "the algorithm" like it’s the boogeyman, all while carrying a smartphone that knows our heart rate, location, and the fact that we’re currently procrastinating in the bathroom. Unless you’re living in a literal Faraday cage and paying in gold bars, Google already knows your soul, your habits, and your favorite pizza topping anyway. So, congratulations on your "private" email—I’m sure the Indian scammers and the data brokers appreciate the extra effort you’re making to keep your spam folder slightly more curated than mine.
