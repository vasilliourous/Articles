Having witnessed my fair share of cloud-based services getting the enshittification treatment with privacy-intrusive ads, unintuitive menus, and paywalled features, I’ve been going through tons of self-hosted utilities to build a FOSS application stack. Although media management tools form a large segment of my local apps, I’ve also followed this notion for my productivity arsenal, as I’d rather not deal with cloud services when I can just host free tools on my home lab nodes. So, here’s the productivity stack that I currently use after years of trial and error.

                        Nextcloud and Blinko lie at the center of my productivity stack
               

            The latter replaced way too many services in my arsenal
    
Nextcloud
 (or rather, NextcloudPi) was one of the first services I’d deployed on my Raspberry Pi 5, as it seemed like a decent platform for uploading and sharing my documents. However, my Nextcloud usage has evolved a lot over the years. Between sharing files with my mates and preserving random documents, I initially used Nextcloud as a backup/private cloud of sorts, without realizing its real utility. You see, Nextcloud has its own app store with a bunch of game-changing tools that transform it from a simple file-sharing platform to a hardcore productivity hub capable of trading blows with the likes of Office 365 and Google Drive.
The Collabora Online and Nextcloud Office apps are my favorite of the bunch, as they let my Nextcloud instance create and edit text documents, spreadsheets, and presentation slides directly from the web UI. Nextcloud also has dedicated tools for creating forms, managing calendar tasks, viewing RSS feeds, accessing recipes, and attending video calls, and although I’ve moved away to other apps for certain tasks, it’s an essential part of my productivity stack.

Speaking of other apps, I’ve yet to encounter something like Blinko for my note-taking needs. While there are note-takers with straightforward UIs, 
Blinko
 packs a minimalist interface with powerful features, all while providing an easy way to create new notes, to-do lists, and memos (or blinkos, as the app calls them). Rather than navigating different directories for flash notes, it lets me jot down ideas as blinkos, and its random walk feature serves as a reminder for fleshing them out later. Once I’ve turned a blinko into a highly-detailed note (or a long checklist) with properly formatted Markdown syntax, I can swap its template with the press of a button, but I can also let it remain in my idea limbo as a memo.
Then you’ve got the LLM-powered features Blinko hides under the hood. RAG search, for instance, lets me feed my own notes into the app and run LLM queries on them, essentially creating a knowledge base without managing multiple folders and directories. I’ve also set automatic summary generation and tag creation, so I can identify all the random, unhinged ideas I jot down after doomscrolling GitHub at 2am. Between its simple note management and powerful idea retrieval facilities, Blinko has easily replaced Obsidian, Google Keep, 
Trilium Notes
, Vikunja, and practically every other note-taker/to-do list builder for my productivity needs.

                        Vaultwarden, Firefly III, and Paperless-ngx (AI and GPT) simplified my life
               

            I’ve got a 3-2-1 backup workflow just to keep these services safe
    
✕
 Remove Ads
Switching gears to applications that save me from a lot of headaches, 
Vaultwarden
 has every quality-of-life feature I could ever need from a password manager, all while running locally on my home server. Besides typical passwords, it can house my TOTP codes, API tokens, passkeys, bank cards, and even identity documents. The best part? Vaultwarden can sync everything to Bitwarden client apps, including browser extensions.
As for my finances, I’ve got a Firefly III instance as my primary bookkeeping service. It lets me track budgets, liabilities, and recurring transactions, and even charts them in detailed graphs. With an extra data importer container, I can feed banking records into Firefly III, and since the app already supports multiple currencies, I can manage my USD, EUR, and INR transactions without manually converting them. I also adore that it doesn’t enforce zero-sum budgeting, as I prefer keeping a flexible budget instead of micro-managing all my finances.
Since 
Paperless-ngx
 is the only reason I haven’t lost any document since my uni days, I consider it just as important as Vaultwarden and Firefly III. I tend to use it with Paperless-GPT for the latter’s LLM-powered OCR provisions, while Paperless AI is responsible for generating tags for my documents and running queries on them. Factor Paperless-ngx’s ability to import files from emails via automated rules, and you can see why I hold it in high regard.

                        Wger and Mealie are my fitness companions
               

            They may not aid me professionally, but I rely on them just the same
    
As a basement dweller who spends most of the time tinkering with server nodes, Wger ensures that I touch grass and get some exercise every once in a while. I’ve used it to create detailed workout plans targeting different muscle groups, and tracking my sets and reps is pretty straightforward thanks to Wger’s built-in exercise templates. Its nutrition planning tools are just as handy, and although Wger has solid recipe management provisions, I tend to rely on Mealie when planning my meals.

After all, Mealie can pull the ingredients and instructions from most recipe websites, and even scale them to meet my nutrition needs. I also prefer its UI over other self-hosted cookbooks and recipe managers, as it’s polished enough to be used by the rest of my household. The fact that it lets me create shopping lists for my entire family is just the cherry on the top.

            The best part? I didn’t spend an extra dime setting up my productivity stack
    
✕

Honestly, building a self-hosted productivity arsenal can have some upfront costs, as you're in charge of both the hardware and the app stack. But if you've got old hardware gathering dust, you can repurpose it into a container-hosting server. Thanks to the lightweight nature of containers, this productivity stack can run well on most systems, including Arm systems like the Raspberry Pi. In fact, I've deployed these services as LXCs on a spare i3-N305 system, but I can even go down to an N100 without major performance issues. The only exceptions to this all-container setup are Nextcloud, which runs as a dedicated virtual machine, and the LLMs powering my Blinko, Paperless-GPT, and Paperless AI instances that remain inside an Ollama LXC deployed on my old PC. But considering that I’m using an old Pascal card for the LLMs, it’s not like I had to pay extra money to grab a high-end graphics card, either. With the right power optimization tweaks, my self-hosting nodes have a minimal impact on my energy bills.
