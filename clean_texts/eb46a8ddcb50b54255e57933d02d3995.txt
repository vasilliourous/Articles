                        I thought local LLMs were just glorified chatbots 
               

            And they seemed like incompetent agents for vibe-coding tasks
    
When I first dabbled in Ollama (and by extension, ChatGPT and other cloud-based AI providers), LLMs didn’t fit into my workflow in any shape or form. Don’t get me wrong: I’d witnessed their reasoning capabilities firsthand, so I understood why everybody was hyping them up. But I had little to no reason to rely on random chatbots to answer my questions. Let’s say I wanted to research a specific topic. An AI model that recites false information with the confidence of a rambling madman becomes pointless when I want cold facts. 
Likewise, I never saw the point in using LLMs to create content, partly because of my pride, and also because I could never trust a clanker with professional journaling tasks. I’ve also had people try to sell me on using AIs for coding, especially since ChatGPT started growing in popularity around the same time I joined my uni. 
✕
 Remove Ads
Now, you might argue that modern-day LLMs can generate fully-functional programs, apps, and websites. But I’ve never been one to board the vibe-coding train. And even when I tried to come up with code snippets on local LLMs, the results were anywhere between somewhat feasible syntax to a complete code spaghetti with random unmet dependencies and inefficient functions. At that point, I’d end up rewriting most of the code or, if it was well beyond my skillset, ditching the project altogether. 
Not to mention, I’ve had a bone to pick with cloud-based models and their paywalled features, constant reliance on external servers, and (most importantly) privacy-intrusive nature, leaving local LLMs as the sole AI providers for my projects. However, I recently began using LLMs differently, and they’re now an essential part of my productivity stack.

                        Anything involving data extraction and analysis is fair game for local LLMs 
               

            Pair them with the right tools, and LLMs can skyrocket your productivity
    
One of the biggest gripes with managing my own media is that I need to perform everything manually, not just the initial setup sequence. Let’s say I wanted to bulk upload bookmarks, documents, or notes to my self-hosted apps. If I’ve already got hundreds of webpages, images, and PDF files stored on these tools, I’ll need to manually create tags to the newly-imported additions, or risk making everything more disorganized. That’s where my AI tools come in handy. Since I’ve got Ollama models capable of sifting through text, I can use them to create tags instantly for the files without doing all the grunt work myself. And that’s pretty much how I’ve been using 
Blinko
, 
Karakeep
, and 
Paperless AI
 (and by extension Paperless-ngx) with my Ollama models for the last couple of months.
✕
 Remove Ads
You see, LLMs excel at processing documents, spreadsheets, and other media files – to the point where I can feed some random files into them and have them summarize their contents. If I had to extract text from some finance-related PDFs, I could just upload them to my Paperless-ngx server, have Paperless GPT leverage my Ollama LLMs, and voilà, I’d get a mostly accurate OCR analysis of the files. Similarly, I can use them to create short summaries of my bookmarks and notes, so I’ll have an easier time finding specific resources when I need them. 

            They’re just as useful for querying private data
    
When it comes to researching specific topics, LLM responses (especially those from weaker models running locally) devolve into a hallucinated mess. However, everything changes when I start adding my own data sources to the mix. Take my 
Open WebUI
 server, for instance. If I wanted to query LLMs about specific files, I can just upload them to the FOSS container and have them come up with precise information every time. The same applies to my 
Open Notebook instance
, where I’ve added several links to official documentation for my DevOps research – and I’ve yet to encounter anything factually incorrect during my questioning sessions.

This doesn’t just apply to spreadsheets or PDFs, either. I may not use my LLMs to generate vibe coded apps, but I’ve started incorporating them into my programming tasks. For example, I can feed log files with thousands of lines to my LLMs, have them pinpoint the blocks where everything went south, and even ask them for troubleshooting advice. Likewise, hooking my LLMs up with Home Assistant lets me chat with them about all the services connected to my smart home hub.

            LLMs can be pretty beneficial once you use them the right way
    
Now, I wouldn’t say that my local LLMs can hold a candle to the reasoning capabilities of high-end AI platforms. But for tedious tasks involving hours of manual effort, local LLMs can be a godsend. And the fact that I can repurpose old GPUs for this task and not worry about paying multiple AI providers is just the cherry on top.
