Home networks are so much more than an ISP connection and a router, today.                         Guest Wi-Fi
               

            Friction, but just in the right dosage
    
Credit:  

 The general concept of a separate guest Wi-Fi network is well-loved by those in the know. It isolates clients on that network, keeps their traffic separate from the home network, and also gives your IoT devices a secret little sandbox. With a good third-party router that's not locked down like the one your ISP is renting out, you could even set up per-client restrictions and rules for peer-to-peer communication on the guest Wi-Fi network.
✕
 Remove Ads
For the advertised use of 
guest Wi-Fi for actual visitors
, the solution works remarkably. However, depending on your router firmware, you might have a simple toggle switch for this feature, or a full-blown voucher generator with expiration dates for each redemption, typically found on Ubiquiti systems or pfSense devices in commercial settings. The former is too open and revealing, while the latter adds too much friction for home users to give visitors access. The ultimate solution lies in making guest Wi-Fi easy to connect to, but hiding it in plain sight by shutting off SSID broadcast and perhaps offering a QR code visitors can use to connect. This way, your IoT devices remain secure at a bad actor's first glance, but you won't need 10 minutes to set up a voucher every time a visitor needs internet.

                        Running that LAN cable
               

            Wired beats wireless for heavy utility
    
On most home networks, I've found an assortment of devices, from smart home hubs, RasPis, NAS machines, and HTPCs. Typically, all but the closest to the router are running on a wireless connection for internet access. While this isn't a bad idea and is unquestionably convenient for the average user who moves homes regularly or is leasing, there is a better way. If you sit down to evaluate which devices need a constant, high-bandwidth, low-latency internet connection, you'll find that a lot of them, besides perhaps your smartphones and tablets, are in fact stationary devices. These include gaming PCs, home servers, NAS units, or even an HTPC/smart TV.
✕
 Remove Ads
Depending on how Ethernet is wired behind your drywall and your comfort level with DIY home repair, you ought to spend the five minutes it takes to run a hardline connection to these devices. At most, you'll need to add a network switch between the router and these devices outnumber the LAN ports on your router. However, the difference a wired line makes is immediate, because you're no longer reliant on device prioritization over the airwaves and QoS settings. 
Running wired backhaul
 to additional nodes in a mesh network is even more important, and five minutes spent running cladding along the baseboard could help your speeds tremendously every day.

                        RJ45 connectors are easily repaired
               

            While we're on the subject of cables
    
Unlike most sturdy connectors like VGA, HDMI, and USB, networking connectors are typically more fragile. RJ45 connector termination on most CAT5 and CAT6a LAN cables shares the same disconnection failsafe as VGA connectors, but instead of a screw, it's typically a flimsy plastic tab you press down. In my experience, plastic doesn't wear well, and after just a few cycles of plugging and unplugging a cable, you'll see stress cracks on this clip before it eventually fails, leaving behind a weak and loose connection. For this reason, network engineers for enterprises might carry a crimping tool in their belt, which makes quick work of replacing these flimsy connectors.
✕
 Remove Ads
While a box of RJ45s is cheap, getting one with a crimper doesn't make much sense if you have access to a 3D printer at home, the community library, a nearby university, or a commercial print service. Every time I place an order for 3D prints, I throw in a few of these 
replacement clips
 for Ethernet cables, just to round off the total. 
These little prints
 sit atop the broken connectors and give them a new lease on life. Moreover, they are far cheaper and easier to replace without specialized tools or buying a new cable. At home, they take about 15 minutes apiece to print out.

                        Replace the ISP DNS
               

            Get yourself a new internet phone book
    
Credit: Docker Hub
 For reasons best known to your ISP, a company-controlled Domain Name Server (DNS) is among the biggest reasons why you're pushed to rent a router instead of using one of your own. This might not be fixable until you break free of that hardware rental situation, but on your own router, there's little stopping you from 
configuring your own DNS server
 with all the benefits like integrated network-wide ad blocking, a private VPN, etc.

Even otherwise, the relatively low-effort option of switching to a public DNS from a reliable provider like Google or Cloudflare comes with significant gains. They advertise greater speed (at least for looking up websites you're visiting), integrated protection against DDoS attacks, and more user data privacy. 
Changing your DNS
 is fairly straightforward in router firmware and takes under a minute for instant network-wide changes. You shouldn't need to reconfigure any clients connected to the home network either. This ought to be the single easiest change with the biggest impact on performance if you're wary of typical misconfiguration pitfalls.

Contrary to popular belief, you don't necessarily have to stick with your ISP-issued hardware if they don't allow these changes. With that out of the way, effecting these changes to your home network shouldn't take long, but they will save you plenty of time waiting for pages to load and troubleshooting issues that might arise.
