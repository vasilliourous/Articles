Running a home lab sounds pretty simple until you actually build out your first setup. Getting the 
home lab
 up and running is only one part of the equation. Containers may or may not update reliably, or might just stay out of date for months because you forgot to trigger a manual update.
✕

That was certainly a problem for me because I didn't want to bother with something like 
WatchTower
, nor did I want blind auto-updates. What I needed was control without friction, so that I could easily update containers on demand, but only with my approval. That's why I ended up installing DockSentry. And it helped me build a setup that was even better than a standard container manager. Now, I can manage my entire container stack directly from Telegram, and it's amazing.

                        A smarter way to manage container updates
               

            A middle ground between manual updates and full automation
    
 I've dabbled in Watchtower before, but it's never really stuck for me because if I'm going to do on-demand container updates anyway, there's really no reason to install yet another container in my stack. DockSentry is different because it takes a completely different approach towards managing updates. Where most 
container manager
 apps take an extreme approach by either updating things automatically in the background, or not doing anything until you manually check in, DockSentry sits somewhere in the middle.
✕
 Remove Ads
It'll watch your containers for updates but won't act on them until you say so. And it does so in a beautifully convenient way. But first, the setup. Getting it up and running is straightforward. You'll find a 
Docker Compose
 file provided on GitHub. Then you generate a Telegram bot token and plug that into the Compose file. That's really all it takes to get it operational. And once it's up, it starts tracking containers right away and surfaces updates as they happen.
When a new image is available, I get a message. From there, I decide what to do. If I've read the changelog, I can choose to update it immediately. If I haven't or if I prefer an older version, I can choose to ignore it for now or come back to it later. That simple loop of managing your containers makes them infinitely easier to manage.
Unlike many other apps, I like how deliberate the updates become. Since the final call on updating a container is still on me, I don't have to worry about broken updates at all, nor am I blindly trusting automation. The app focuses on giving me visibility first and then control. Interestingly enough, despite the fairly manual approach, DockerSentry even has a safety net built in. If an update goes wrong, I can easily revert to the previous version with a single Telegram command. That alone makes me far more comfortable keeping things up to date.

                        Manage your stack directly from Telegram
               

            No more jumping between dashboards
    
✕
 Remove Ads
 The real advantage of DockSentry is in how you interact with it. Instead of opening up a dashboard every time you want to check something, everything goes via the Telegram bot.
On the face of it, it might sound like a novelty, but it doesn't take too long for it to become the fastest way to manage your stack. For one, I don't need to log 
in to 
Portainer
 just to check
 if updates are available. I don't need to navigate through containers to trigger an action. It's all right there in the chat interface.
It has a profound impact on how often you actually maintain your system. Instead of setting aside time to check on things, you handle updates as they come in. It turns maintenance into something you can manage from practically anywhere, rather than a chore you keep putting off until Sunday.
It's also useful for handling different types of containers, well, differently. Not every container has to be updated simultaneously. Some services can be updated frequently enough, but for my critical services, I exercise a bit more caution. With DockSentry, I can choose what to update and when, rather than applying a one-size-fits-all strategy across my stack.
While DockSentry isn't really built with a 
web interface
 in mind, one is available for a quick overview or to change settings, but I've rarely needed it. Telegram ends up being the primary interface simply because it's faster, always available from everywhere.
There are a few other features too, like the ability to pin apps that you don't want updated and the ability for the bot to auto-update.
✕
 Remove Ads

            A better way to manage your containers
    
DockSentry fits perfectly into my setup as a layer on top of existing tools. While it can spin up containers too, I don't use it for that. I still use Portainer for 
configuring containers
 and handling more specific changes. But for everyday management and updates, DockSentry takes over.
The most impactful change is in how little effort it takes to stay on top of things. Updates can be managed right from your phone without having to pop open your laptop or log in from a desktop. More importantly, I'm not worried about missing something important. If there's a critical update, I can trigger it from my phone without waiting, without giving up necessary control.
Managing a 
homelab
 often means choosing between convenience and control. DockSentry removes that trade-off. It gives you simplicity and control, and I'd highly recommend giving it a shot in your home lab.

    						DockerSentry
			
DockerSentry is a Docker container manager that lets you monitor, approve and control container updates from Telegram.
