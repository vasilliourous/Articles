Most wikis and documentation platforms I've used are flexible. The downside, though, is that they tend to become messy very quickly. If you work across machines, it's easy to lose track of documentation when there are several nested pages, databases, and scattered notes.

However, when I tried using BookStack, there was a key difference rooted in a philosophy that lets you organize knowledge like real books. This design forces me to structure my thinking clearly, making it easier to store and organize information. 
I have used several open-source knowledge management tools
, but BookStack stands out.

                        The four-level structure that quietly fixes messy documentation
               

            The shelves-books-chapters-pages model changes how you organize knowledge
    
You'll notice what makes 
BookStack
 stand out when you create your first note. It organizes content into a strict hierarchy: Shelves, Books, Chapters, then Pages. These categorizations function just as you expect. Shelves are at the top and can hold multiple books. I use them for broad topics.
Then Books come next and may hold major subjects. I use Chapters to divide my Books into multiple sections, while my actual documentation entries go into Pages. Because chapters are optional, I can skip that level for very small topics and create a page directly.
BookStack handles topic overlap by allowing books to span multiple Shelves. This is a practical feature that eliminates duplication. For example, my Docker Setup Guide appears on both my Linux and Home Lab Shelves. Once you understand the BookStack segmentation below, organization is easy:

This structure is the key difference between BookStack and several other apps, like Notion. It avoids the endless nested folders and makes it harder to keep dumping notes into just one knowledge base. Even months after creating an entry, it's easy to locate because entries are stored in a predictable structure.

                        Writing documentation becomes easier when the editor disappears
               

            The WYSIWYG editor makes technical documentation easier to maintain
    
Documenting and formatting can often conflict, and good documentation tools separate these two layers. That's what BookStack does. If you navigate to 
Settings ->
Customization
, under the 
Default Page Editor
 section, you can choose
 WYSIWYG
, 
Markdown
, or the 
New WYSIWYG (in beta)
.
WYSIWYG is the default option and offers an interface similar to what you get on many standard word processors. It's handy because I can paste screenshots and commands. It allows me to worry less about formatting syntax as I build structured documentation. Markdown, on the other hand, allows a plain text workflow.

However, the integration with 
diagrams.net
 is one element that makes documentation with this tool come to life. 
Diagrams.net
 lets me create diagrams right from the editor. This is great when I'm designing network layouts or creating simple workflows.
You can also enable a changelog for your pages, which I find super helpful for keeping track of minor details. A tiny note in my change log records my last edits and ensures that even when I return to my page after several months, I do not lose context.

                        The features that make it viable as a real knowledge base
               

            Built-in permissions, search, and revisions make collaboration possible
    
Afam Onyimadu / MUO
 Although I use BookStack for personal notes, the real advantage is the ease of scaling it into a shared knowledge base. It includes full-text search across the entire system. This makes finding solutions easy, as all you have to do is search for a command or error message.
I get the best out of related documentation by linking pages to other pages or to specific paragraphs within a page. Also, the version history is an integral fail-safe. When I accidentally remove an item or if something breaks, restoring an earlier version typically fixes the problem. It also includes a comprehensive image management system that keeps my screenshots organized and easily retrievable. However, one standout feature for teams is its role and permission system. I control who has access to different Books, Chapters, and Pages by giving flexible access rights to team members.

Deploying BookStack is quite straightforward, even though it may require some comfort with the command line. You can choose to install it directly on a server or run it through Docker. It has lightweight requirements, making self-hosting an option to 
repurposing an old laptop
.

                        Where BookStack fits among other knowledge tools
               

            Not every note-taking app solves the same problem
    
Afam Onyimadu / MUO
 BookStack is unique and addresses a different problem than most note-taking apps. Notion is a flexible workspace for task databases, and project planning. 
Obsidian, which is one of my favorite note-taking apps
, builds its system around linking ideas together. But BookStack focuses on being a structured documentation system.

            Structure forces clarity
    
In the end, the big win for BookStack is the discipline its structure creates. What may have been a scattered troubleshooting note on some platforms became a well-organized and easily accessible note. It may not sound like a big shift, but it pushes you to document in a way someone else might read later.
